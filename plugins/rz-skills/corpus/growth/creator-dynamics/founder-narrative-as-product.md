# Founder Narrative as Product — The Story IS the Strategy

## What it is
The principle: every creator brand has an underlying narrative arc — the story that explains who the creator is, where they came from, what they're moving toward. This narrative is not marketing copy; it's the spine that every artifact (post, article, talk, conversation) either reinforces or undermines. For Riché's brand, the narrative arc is explicit and load-bearing. Content that fits the arc compounds the brand; content that doesn't fit the arc — even if individually good — dilutes it.

## Riché's narrative arc (the explicit version)

**Past:** Senior PM at Suzy who shipped consumer-research products that reached scale (~30M consumers). Learned what context-architecture failures look like in production at consumer scale.

**Present:** Building Helm Labs (the experiment lab) and richezamor.com (the practitioner thought-leadership platform). Translating production AI-PM experience into frameworks for the broader Product community. Speaking at PM events; writing the Context Layer thesis.

**Future:** AI Product VP/CPO at a company building the next generation of LLM-powered consumer or B2B systems. The brand work positions him for that role specifically — credibility with hiring managers (Segment 1), peer recognition with Product leaders (Segment 2), connectivity to founders (Segment 3), reach via event organizers (Segment 4).

**The throughline:** practitioner who's done it, distilled the lessons, helps others figure out the same problems, ready to do it again at greater scale.

This is the arc every artifact should fit.

## Why it matters
Without an explicit narrative arc, content drifts. Every post becomes a standalone artifact judged on its own merits. Some are good; some aren't; none compound into a recognizable brand position. The audience can't form a clear mental model of "who is this person and why should I care?" because each piece is self-contained.

With an explicit narrative arc, every artifact is judged twice: on its own merits AND on whether it fits the arc. Content that fits compounds — Segment 1 hiring managers and Segment 2 peers build a coherent picture across 3-5 touchpoints. Content that doesn't fit either dilutes the picture (best case) or contradicts it (worst case).

The narrative arc is also the answer to "why does this brand exist and where is it going?" — the question that determines whether someone bookmarks, shares, hires, or invites you to their podcast. Brands with clear arcs get that benefit; brands without don't.

For Riché specifically, the arc is what differentiates from the saturated "AI-PM thought leader" category. The category is generic; the arc is specific (Suzy → Helm Labs → next role; consumer-AI-PM-with-production-scars; practitioner not theorist). Anything that drifts from the arc surrenders that differentiation.

## How to apply

**The narrative-fit check (applied to every artifact):**

Before publishing any post, article, talk, or even substantial comment, ask:
1. **Does this reinforce a piece of the arc?** (Past: "I learned X at Suzy"; Present: "Here's how I'm thinking about Y at Helm Labs"; Future: "The kind of work I want to do next")
2. **Or is it neutral/orthogonal?** (A general PM observation that doesn't connect to the arc — usually OK if rare; suspicious if common)
3. **Or does it contradict the arc?** (Generic AI/PM commentary that could come from anyone; positions Riché as a generalist when the arc is specific)

**Categories of content sorted by arc-fit:**

**Reinforcing (publish freely):**
- Suzy-derived lessons (consumer research, scale-context failures, prioritization at scale)
- Helm Labs experiments (technical depth on context architecture, AI eval frameworks)
- Context Layer thesis development (the moat)
- Practitioner-not-theorist contrast pieces
- Specific frameworks Riché has invented or refined
- Interviews/podcasts where the arc gets told to a new audience

**Neutral (publish selectively, ≤20% of cadence):**
- General PM career observations
- Industry takes on AI-PM news
- Tooling reviews (only if connected to the practitioner positioning)

**Orthogonal (publish rarely):**
- Personal hobby content (photography, NYC food, family — bounded per `corpus/growth/creator-dynamics/vulnerability-vs-authority-balance.md`)
- Holiday/seasonal posts
- Generic "happy Friday" type content

**Contradicting (don't publish):**
- AI-as-magic hype that contradicts the practitioner-not-theorist positioning
- "Anyone can do this with no experience" content (dilutes the practitioner credentialing)
- Leadership-platitudes content disconnected from product specifics (dilutes the AI-PM positioning)

**The narrative arc and channel strategy:**

The arc determines which channels matter. Channels that reach Segments 1 and 2 (LinkedIn primarily) reinforce the arc to the hiring/peer audience. Channels that reach Segment 3 (X, HN, GitHub) reinforce the arc to the founder audience. Channels that don't reach any of the 4 segments (TikTok) can't reinforce the arc, which is one of several reasons they're declined.

**Quarterly narrative review:**

At each quarterly review, audit the prior quarter's content for arc-fit:
- % reinforcing: target ≥60%
- % neutral: target ≤25%
- % orthogonal: target ≤10%
- % contradicting: target 0%

If the mix drifts (especially: % reinforcing dropping), the next quarter's planning explicitly biases toward reinforcing content.

## Examples
1. **Reinforcing (works).** A /thinking article: "What I learned shipping Suzy's recommendations system to 30M consumers." Past arc; specific scale; specific lesson; reinforces practitioner credentialing. High peer reshare; cited in 2 follow-up podcast invites.
2. **Neutral OK (occasional use).** A LinkedIn post commenting on Anthropic's latest model release. Doesn't reinforce the arc but doesn't contradict it. Posted because the news matters; one such post per quarter is fine.
3. **Orthogonal bounded (works).** A Friday story post mentions a quiet weekend before pivoting to a Suzy lesson. Personal note bounded; arc reinforced. Doesn't shift brand category.
4. **Contradicting avoided.** A peer asks Riché to co-author a "10 AI-PM tips for beginners" piece. Politely declines: contradicts the practitioner-not-tips-merchant positioning. Suggests an alternative collaboration on a deeper architecture topic that fits both their arcs.

## Anti-patterns
- Posting good content that doesn't fit the arc, repeatedly. Quality without arc-fit doesn't compound.
- Letting orthogonal content drift above 10% of cadence. Brand category starts shifting; audience expectations dilute.
- Pivoting the arc without intentional re-positioning. If the arc changes (e.g., Riché takes a CPO role), the narrative needs explicit updating; quiet drift confuses the audience.
- Treating the arc as marketing copy. The arc is editorial; it filters every piece, not just the bio.
