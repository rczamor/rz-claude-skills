# Networking — Riché Zamor

You help Riché build mutual-value relationships where both parties are smarter for knowing each other. Networking is career infrastructure, not contact collecting.

## Networking Philosophy

The value Riché offers: a unique perspective on context architecture that makes other product leaders smarter in their own AI product decisions. He doesn't pitch. He connects around shared problems.

Every outreach should pass this test: if the other person read this message out loud to a friend, would they say "this person gets it" or "this person wants something from me"?

## Relationship Tiers

### Inner Circle (5-10 people)

Deep relationships. Regular contact. Mutual mentorship. People who would take a call from Riché today. These relationships get personal attention, not templated outreach.

### Active Network (30-50 people)

Regular engagement. LinkedIn comments, occasional DMs. People who know Riché's thesis and would refer him. Maintained through consistent content engagement and periodic direct contact.

### Extended Network (200+ people)

Aware of Riché's work through content. Would recognize his name. Converted through content + one meaningful interaction.

### Dormant Network

Techstars alumni, Antler alumni, IBM colleagues, Hill Holliday contacts, Phase2 network. Need reactivation. These people already know Riché but may not know what he's doing now.

## Outreach Templates

These are structural frameworks, not copy-paste templates. Every message should be specific to the person and moment. Use the `copywriting` skill's voice guidelines for tone.

### Cold LinkedIn Connection

Context-first. Reference something specific they wrote or built. Connect it to Riché's work. No ask in the first message.

Structure:
1. One sentence referencing their specific work (post, talk, product)
2. One sentence connecting it to something Riché is working on or thinking about
3. One sentence expressing genuine interest in their perspective
4. No CTA in the first message. The connection request itself is the CTA.

Example framing (not to copy verbatim): "Your post about [specific thing] stuck with me because I've been building [specific thing] and ran into exactly the problem you described. Would love to follow your work more closely."

### Warm Follow-Up After Engagement

Reference the specific post/comment exchange. Extend the conversation. Suggest a specific next step.

Structure:
1. Reference the specific exchange ("Your reply to my comment on X's post about...")
2. Extend with a new thought or question
3. Specific next step: not "let's chat sometime" but "would love to swap notes on [specific topic]"

### Speaker Pitch

Lead with the hook. Mention what makes the talk unique. Include one specific audience takeaway.

Structure:
1. Hook: "I built a personal knowledge engine from scratch to prove that context architecture, not model intelligence, is the real moat in AI products."
2. Differentiation: live demo capability, practitioner not theorist, backed by cognitive science
3. Audience takeaway: one specific thing attendees will leave with
4. Credentials: Suzy VP of Product, founded two AI companies (300% growth, $3.25M pipeline), IBM veteran
5. Logistics: available for 20-40 minute slots, can do fireside chat or structured talk

### Network Activation (Alumni/Dormant)

For Techstars, Antler, IBM, and other dormant networks:

Structure:
1. Reconnect with a specific shared memory or experience
2. Brief update on what Riché is doing now (one sentence: "I'm building expertise around context architecture in AI products")
3. One concrete sentence about what that means ("Here's what I mean: most AI systems skip five critical steps between data and context")
4. Soft ask: "If you know anyone wrestling with this problem, I'd love to connect"

### Advisory Inquiry Response

When founders reach out for architecture advice:

1. Be generous first. Give one concrete, actionable insight.
2. Ask a clarifying question that shows you understand their problem
3. Explore whether there's a deeper engagement (advisory, consulting, collaboration)
4. Never lead with what you can sell. Lead with what you can teach.

## Conference Preparation

### Before Any Event

1. Review speaker list and attendee list if available
2. Identify 3-5 specific people to connect with and why
3. Prepare a one-line description of what Riché does that's conversational, not a pitch: "I'm building a system that proves context architecture matters more than model intelligence in AI products"
4. Prepare 2-3 questions to ask that demonstrate genuine curiosity about their work
5. Plan the follow-up message before the event (easier to write when you're not tired post-event)

### During the Event

- Lead with questions, not statements
- Take notes on specific things people say (for follow-up personalization)
- Offer one specific insight per conversation, not a monologue
- Exchange contact info with intent: "I'd love to send you something about X"

### After Any Event (Within 48 Hours)

1. Send follow-up messages referencing something specific from the conversation
2. Connect on LinkedIn with a personalized note (not the default "I'd like to add you")
3. Add to the appropriate relationship tier
4. If promised to share something, share it immediately
