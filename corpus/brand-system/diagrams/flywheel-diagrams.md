# Flywheel Diagrams

## What it is
Circular diagrams showing reinforcing loops — used for audience development and content strategy visuals. Each node in the flywheel feeds the next, and the system gains momentum as it cycles. Pairs directly with the growth-marketing skill's flywheel model.

The structural argument: this isn't a funnel (one-time top-to-bottom). It's a wheel (each turn makes the next turn easier).

## Why it matters
Flywheels are the visual counterpart to Riché's growth thesis. A funnel diagram says "I capture, you convert." A flywheel says "every cycle compounds." That's the right metaphor for personal brand growth, content distribution, and audience-building loops — and the visual style reinforces the message every time it appears next to a post about audience strategy.

## How to apply
Build the flywheel in this structure:

1. **Circular arrangement of 3–6 nodes.** Three to six is the sweet spot. Fewer and it doesn't look like a system; more and it gets unreadable.
2. **Each node is a labeled container with a verb-noun action.** "Ship evidence" → "Earn attention" → "Grow audience" → "Validate work" → back to "Ship evidence." Verbs make the loop active.
3. **Arrows curving along the circumference.** Use a consistent stroke width (1.5–2px) and a slight thickness ramp toward the arrowhead so direction is unambiguous.
4. **Center label.** A short noun phrase in JetBrains Mono 16pt at the center: e.g., "AUDIENCE FLYWHEEL" or "CONTENT COMPOUNDING LOOP."
5. **Apply the accent rule.** The accent color goes on the arrow currently being emphasized in the post (e.g., if the post is about "ship evidence", that node and its outgoing arrow get the accent). Other nodes stay in white/gray.
6. **Negative space inside the circle.** Don't crowd the center with extra graphics. The empty interior makes the loop legible.

**Format variants:**
- **Slide format**: Larger center label, fuller node descriptions.
- **Social square**: Compress node labels to 2–3 words each, expand the center label.
- **Inline blog**: Smaller render, use as a section anchor rather than a hero image.

## Examples
1. **LinkedIn post on the audience growth flywheel.** Five-node flywheel: Ship → Post → Engage → Convert (warm intros) → Validate. Emerald-green accent on "Ship → Post" arrow because the post argues that publishing evidence is the missed step.
2. **Talk slide on content compounding.** Four-node flywheel inside a section break slide: Hook → Diagram → Comment → Republish. Center: "CONTENT COMPOUNDING LOOP." No accent — the flywheel is the slide.
3. **Speaker one-sheet visual.** Compact three-node flywheel showing how speaking → audience → speaking opportunities. Used as a small visual on the speaker page.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating a flywheel as a checklist. If the nodes are sequential phases that don't actually feed back, draw a flow, not a wheel. Don't use the metaphor where it doesn't fit.
- Adding decorative gears or "spinning" motion lines. The geometric circle and the curved arrows are enough — the metaphor doesn't need illustration.
