# One Idea Per Slide

## What it is
Each slide carries exactly one idea. If a slide needs a paragraph to explain it, that's two slides. If a slide is hosting two distinct claims connected by a "but" or "however," that's two slides. The slide-count consequence: a 20-minute conference talk lands at 25–35 slides — more slides with less per slide is the right trade.

## Why it matters
Audiences process one new idea every few seconds. A slide with three ideas competes against itself — the audience is still parsing point one when point two arrives. The speaker, meanwhile, has to choose which point to emphasize, and the slide has already cued the audience that all three matter equally. Splitting into separate slides lets the speaker control pacing, lets each idea breathe, and makes recordings easier to clip into shareable segments.

## How to apply
- **Read the slide aloud.** If you find yourself saying "and also" or "but more importantly" — split.
- **Test the headline alone.** If the slide's headline alone doesn't carry the idea, the slide is doing too many jobs.
- **Don't fear slide count.** A 35-slide deck with one idea each is more confident than a 15-slide deck with three ideas each. Confidence reads as "I know exactly what I want you to take from each beat."
- **Build, don't combine.** If two ideas need to appear together, use a build animation — the second idea reveals after the first lands. Same slide structure, but the audience processes them in sequence.

## Examples
1. **Bad: a slide titled "RAG vs. Context Layer" with three columns of text comparing them.** The audience has to read three columns while the speaker is trying to set context. **Good: three slides** — slide 1 shows the RAG side alone, slide 2 reveals the Context Layer side, slide 3 names the gap.
2. **Bad: a "Five Dimensions of Context Quality" slide listing all five with definitions.** The audience scans the list while the speaker introduces dimension one. **Good: six slides** — one cover slide, then one slide per dimension.
3. **Bad: a "Lessons Learned" slide with seven bullets.** **Good: cut to the two lessons that actually matter, give them a slide each, drop the other five.**

## Anti-patterns
- Combining slides "to keep the deck short." The 30-second-per-slide pacing is the goal; a shorter deck with denser slides is worse, not better.
- Treating a slide as a leave-behind document. The deck is a performance tool — distribute a written summary separately for anyone who wants the prose.
