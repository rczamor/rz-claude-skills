# No Bullet Walls

## What it is
Slides with stacked bullet lists are banned. If the content needs to be a list, render it visually — icons in a row, labeled cards in a grid, a flow diagram, a comparison table. The bullet point is the lowest-information visual choice; the slide always loses to a structural alternative.

## Why it matters
A bullet list signals that the speaker hasn't done the work of choosing what matters. Six bullets means six things, all weighted equally — which means the audience has nothing to anchor on. Rendering the same content as a visual structure forces a hierarchy: which item gets the accent, which item is in the center, which item is shaped differently. The structural choice is the thinking. Bullets skip the thinking.

## How to apply
- **If a list has 3 items: render as a row of three cards or three icons with labels.** Each item gets equal visual weight, but the structure (cards, geometric shapes) is the brand.
- **If a list has 4–6 items: render as a 2x2 or 2x3 grid, or as a horizontal flow if the items are sequential.** Use icons from a single library; avoid bullets.
- **If a list has more than 6 items: cut it down or split across slides.** Six is already pushing the audience's working memory; nine is a wall.
- **If the list is sequential: use arrows, not bullets.** Sequential bullet lists hide the sequence — turn them into a flow.
- **If the list is hierarchical (nested bullets): collapse to top level, then dig into each on its own slide.** Nested bullets are a confession that one slide is doing too many jobs.

## Examples
1. **Bad: a slide titled "Five Steps of Context Generation" with five bullet points.** **Good: the five-step flow diagram** — same five items, but the diagram structure is the argument.
2. **Bad: a slide titled "What We Learned" with seven bullets.** **Good: pick the two lessons that matter, give each its own slide with a one-line headline.**
3. **Bad: a slide titled "Pros and Cons" with two columns of bullets.** **Good: a comparison table where the cells are visual** (checkmark / X-mark glyphs in monospace, accent color on the differentiating row).

## Anti-patterns
- "But the conference template requires bullets." Override the template. The brand identity is the priority; if the conference enforces a template, deliver the deck in the template but keep the bullet count to one per slide and use visuals as the dominant element.
- Hidden bullet walls — a slide with five bullets, but each bullet is short. Still a wall. Render it visually.
