# Minimal Text, Maximum Impact

## What it is
The slide provides the anchor. The speaker provides the explanation. Slides are not teleprompters and they are not handouts — they are visual reinforcement for what the audience is hearing. The rule of thumb: a slide should be readable in under three seconds.

## Why it matters
When a slide has paragraphs of text, the audience reads the slide instead of listening. The speaker becomes a narrator of their own slides — which means the speaker is competing with the slides for the audience's attention. Minimal text inverts that dynamic: the audience listens, glances at the slide, and the slide locks the idea in. The speaker becomes the source of the argument; the slide becomes the proof.

## How to apply
- **Three-second rule.** A slide should be parseable in under three seconds. If it takes longer, cut text or split into multiple slides.
- **Headline + visual.** Default slide structure: a headline (Inter SemiBold 48–64pt) and a single visual element (diagram, code snippet, or single sentence). That's the whole slide.
- **No subheads, no captions.** If a slide has a headline, a subhead, and a caption under the visual — the subhead and the caption belong in the speaker's mouth, not on the slide.
- **Cut every word that isn't load-bearing.** "The Five Dimensions of Context Quality" → "Context Quality Framework." Same idea, fewer words to parse.
- **Speaker notes carry the prose.** Anything the speaker needs to remember goes in the notes pane — never on the slide.

## Examples
1. **Strong: a slide with the headline "Data is not context." in Inter Bold 64pt, centered on `#0a0a0a`. Nothing else.** The speaker gets 30 seconds to unpack it.
2. **Strong: a slide with the diagram alone, no headline.** When the visual is the argument, the speaker introduces it verbally and lets it stand.
3. **Weak: a slide titled "Why Context Matters" with five bullet points each starting with "Because..."** The bullets are the speech. Cut them — say them.

## Anti-patterns
- "But the deck will be shared after — people who weren't there need the context." Distribute a written summary or a recorded talk. Don't compromise the live deck for the leave-behind audience.
- Adding "speaker quotes" as decoration. If the quote is the speaker's, just say it. If it's someone else's, attribute it minimally and move on.
