# Friday Stories

## What it is
A variable-length reflective post — usually 350-700 words — that tells a real story chronologically and ends with a distilled lesson. Honest, warm, character-driven. The story must be Riché's: a moment from IBM, Helm Labs, Suzy, the early Grandstage days, the Boston Beer Microsoft Access project in 2003, the move to AI product leadership. The lesson at the end is one sentence. The story does the heavy lifting; the lesson just names what the reader already feels.

## Why it matters
Friday is when feeds quiet down and personal posts outperform technical ones. It's also when Segment 4 (event organizers, podcast hosts) browse for speakers with a voice. A good story humanizes the architecture posts from Mon-Thu. It also provides the connective tissue for the brand: Riché isn't just a system; he's a person who had to learn this. The Friday slot is where the Leadership 20% domain lives — what he's earned, what he's broken, what he's rebuilt.

## How to apply
1. Open with a moment, not a thesis. A specific Tuesday afternoon, a specific Slack message, a specific door he walked through.
2. Tell the story in chronological order. Resist the urge to flash back or start in medias res — those are screenwriter moves; they don't fit the voice.
3. Name people only when it adds substance, never to name-drop. Use first-name or pseudonym for non-public figures.
4. End with one sentence of distilled lesson. No "key takeaway" header. No bullet list. Just the line.
5. Voice test: would Riché tell this story to a friend on a long flight? If it sounds rehearsed, rewrite.

## Examples

**Topic: The Microsoft Access project at Boston Beer in 2003.** Opens with the moment Riché realized the data platform he'd built was the only system the operations team trusted. Walks through the year. Closes: "I didn't know it was a context system. I thought it was a database. Twenty years later I'm still building the same thing — just with better tools."

**Topic: Hanging up the towel at IBM.** A story about leaving the legacy track to chase AI product work. The moment of decision, the conversation with the manager, the first three months of doubt. Closes: "I didn't quit IBM. I quit the version of me that was waiting for permission."

**Topic: The Helm Labs pre-launch pipeline that wasn't.** Honest about the $3.25M pre-launch pipeline being a pipeline, not revenue, and what that taught him about the difference between signal and substance. Closes with a line on confusing intent with outcome.

**Topic: The first Grandstage user who churned.** The post-mortem, the embarrassment, the rebuild. Closes: "300% user growth started the day I stopped optimizing for the demo and started optimizing for the second session."

## Anti-patterns
- Story as humblebrag. "I almost didn't take the IBM offer..." that ends with how brilliant the decision was. Real stories include the cost.
- Lesson before story. If the post opens "I learned that..." you're writing an essay, not telling a story. Lead with the moment.
- Generic "lessons from my career" wrap-up. Each Friday Story is one moment, not a retrospective montage.
