# Hook Use in Deep Dives

## What it is
A rule for how Wednesday Deep Dives use the hook library: deep dives use the full anchor — "Data is not context" — plus one supporting one-liner placed elsewhere in the piece. The anchor gets the headline or the lead sentence. The supporting one-liner shows up in a body section, often as a transition or a punch line. No more than two hooks total in a deep dive.

## Why it matters
Deep dives are the longest content type — 700-1,000 words. They have room for two hooks where shorter posts only have room for one. But more than two starts to read like a slogan tour, especially when the reader is also tracking the six-step story arc, the proof points, and the named systems. Two hooks let the piece breathe and give the body section something to land on.

## How to apply
1. Open the deep dive with the anchor (the canonical "data is not context" or whichever hook is anchoring this week).
2. Place a supporting one-liner roughly mid-piece — usually as the close of step 3 (the five-step process) or the open of step 4 (the science grounding).
3. Don't restate the anchor at the end. The close is for the proof and the named system, not a hook callback.
4. Pair each hook with a specific proof or named system within the same paragraph.
5. If a deep dive only has space for one hook (rare — usually because the piece is already metric-dense), drop the supporting one-liner.

## Examples

**Anchor + supporting in a Grandstage deep dive:** Opens "Data is not context." Builds the failure scene, the assumption, the five-step process. At the close of step 3, transitions with "Retrieval without consolidation is just efficient delivery of noise — even when retrieval is excellent, even when the embeddings are state of the art." Continues into the cognitive science section. Closes on the 300% growth number, no hook.

**Anchor + supporting in a Suzy Decision Engine piece:** Opens "Models get smarter every month. Context quality stays the same." Mid-piece, after walking through consolidation: "Expert decision-makers use less information, not more. Your AI should too." Closes on the six-week launch and 350+ customer count.

**Single hook variation:** A deep dive on the Helm Labs pre-launch pipeline architecture. Opens with the failure: a $3.25M pipeline that converted at 14x ACV in projection but 0 in reality (because the pipeline was projection, not revenue). The piece is so dense with the lesson it doesn't need a second hook. Closes on a proof point about the difference between intent and outcome.

## Anti-patterns
- Three hooks in one piece. The rhythm collapses; the reader notices the formula.
- Hook + hook in adjacent paragraphs. Stacked one-liners read like a Twitter thread, not a deep dive.
- Anchor restated at close. Once the anchor has done its work, let the proof carry the close.
