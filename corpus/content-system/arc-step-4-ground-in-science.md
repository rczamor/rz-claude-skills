# Arc Step 4 — Ground in Science

## What it is
Step 4 of the context generation story arc: a single, selectively-chosen cognitive science or information theory insight that grounds the five-step process in something deeper than craft preference. One insight per piece. Not a literature review. Not three connected concepts. One. The point is to give the reader a reason to believe the framework will keep working after they leave the post.

## Why it matters
Without this step, the framework looks like Riché's preference. With it, the framework looks like a translation of how expert decision-making actually works. This is the move that distinguishes practitioner-grounded thought leadership from frameworks-of-the-week. It also reinforces the voice identity: someone who reads cognitive science and translates it into product architecture, not someone who quotes papers to look smart.

## How to apply
1. Pick one insight. Common choices: Miller's 7±2 (working memory limits), expert vs. novice information use (experts use less, more structured), Klein's recognition-primed decision making, dual-process theory, Shannon's information theory on signal vs. noise.
2. State the insight in plain language. No jargon, no citation chains. One short paragraph.
3. Connect the insight to one of the five steps. "Consolidate works because expert decision-makers use less information, not more — they hold compressed schemas, not raw data."
4. Stop. Do not extend into a second insight. The point is selectivity.
5. Length: 75-125 words. Shorter than other sections by design.

## Examples

**Miller's 7±2 grounding the consolidation step:** "Miller's classic finding — working memory holds about seven items, plus or minus two — is why consolidation matters more than retrieval. The model has a much larger window than a person. But quality scales with structure, not size. Four well-consolidated chunks outperform forty raw documents because attention is finite even at scale. The model isn't recreating human cognition; it's failing in the same shape."

**Expert vs. novice information use grounding prioritization:** "Studies of expert decision-makers — chess masters, ER doctors, fund managers — keep finding the same pattern: experts use less information than novices, not more. They hold compressed schemas. They ignore signals novices fixate on. The prioritization step is built on this finding. Decision-readiness ranks the way an expert would: what would change my answer? What's just noise that looks important?"

**Shannon information theory grounding curation:** "Shannon defined information as the reduction of uncertainty. Most data isn't information by that definition — it's noise that happens to be measurable. Curation is the step where you decide what counts as information for your specific question. Skipping it means filling the prompt with measurable-but-uninformative content."

## Anti-patterns
- Multiple insights in one piece. Two cognitive science citations dilute each other and make the post feel like a lecture. One insight per piece, full stop.
- Citing without translating. "Per Miller (1956)..." with a paper reference but no plain-language version is academic theater. Translate first.
- Faking the insight. If Riché hasn't actually read the source, don't cite it. The voice depends on the insight being real.
