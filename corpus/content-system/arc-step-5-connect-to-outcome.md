# Arc Step 5 — Connect to Outcome
domain: content-system

## What it is
Step 5 of the context generation story arc: tie the architectural choice to a business outcome. A specific metric, a UX shift, a defensibility argument, or a unit-economics change. This is where the post earns its place in a senior PM's bookmark folder. Without it, the deep dive reads like an architecture diagram — interesting, not useful. With it, the deep dive becomes the link a reader sends to their CTO.

## Why it matters
Riché's positioning is practitioner-first: every claim grounds in something that shipped and produced a measurable result. Step 5 is where that positioning gets its proof. It also closes the loop from the failure scene at step 1 — the same kind of moment, but now with the right architecture and a different outcome. The reader sees the before/after.

## How to apply
1. Pick one outcome dimension: metric, UX, defensibility, or unit economics. Don't try for all four in one post.
2. State the outcome with a specific number or named change. "300% user growth," "$0 CAC," "context size reduced 4x with no quality loss," "support deflection rate up from 22% to 41%."
3. Attribute the outcome to the architecture choice — not as causation theater, but as a directionally honest claim. "After the rebuild, growth shifted." "Six weeks after the Decision Engine launch, NPS moved 18 points."
4. Use only proof points from the canonical inventory: Grandstage, Helm Labs, IBM, Suzy, Context Layer Engine. Don't invent.
5. Length: 100-175 words. Concrete and confident; no hedging.

## Examples

**Grandstage outcome:** "After the rebuild, two things shifted at once. User growth went from flat to 300% over the next quarter. Customer acquisition cost stayed at zero — every new user came from another user telling them. The architecture didn't cause both numbers. But the architecture is what made it possible for the product to keep its word at scale. A retrieval system that lies once costs a referral. A context system that decides well buys one."

**Suzy outcome:** "The Decision Engine shipped in six weeks. Three pillars — Intelligence, Insights, Impact — mapped to the consolidation, prioritization, and storage steps. Within the first quarter, 350+ enterprise customers were running on the new architecture. The metric the executive team cared about was time-to-decision: how long from a customer asking a question to having an answer they'd act on. That number dropped 60%."

**IBM outcome (composite):** "Personalization built on the same five-step pipeline produced millions in revenue across three product lines. Not because the model was better — the model was the same. Because the context that reached it was finally answering the question the customer asked, not the question our schema was prepared for."

## Anti-patterns
- Vague outcomes. "Better results" or "improved performance" — useless. Numbers or named changes only.
- Inventing numbers. If a claim isn't in the canonical proof inventory, it doesn't go in a post. Ever.
- Causation theater. "This architecture caused 300% growth" overclaims. "After the rebuild, growth shifted to 300%" is honest about correlation.
