# Newsletter Draft Format

## What it is
A long-form newsletter format, monthly cadence, deferred to Q3 2026 pending ~1,500-2,000 LinkedIn followers. The newsletter is the consolidation channel: it pulls the month's strongest deep dives, signals, and stories into one ~2,500-word issue with original connective material. It's the format that turns a stream of posts into a body of work — and the asset that travels through forwarding and bookmarking long after the LinkedIn algorithm has moved on.

## Why it matters
LinkedIn posts have a 48-hour half-life. A great deep dive disappears from the feed by Friday. The newsletter rescues that work by re-presenting it to subscribers who opted in specifically for the long form. It also creates a different distribution loop: forwarded emails reach Segment 1 hiring managers in a way LinkedIn posts can't (executives don't scroll LinkedIn; they read forwarded newsletters from peers they trust). The format is being designed now so it's ready to launch when the audience reaches scale.

## How to apply

1. **Issue structure (one issue/month, ~2,500 words):**
   - **Cold open (~150 words):** A single moment from the month. Often a conversation, a meeting, a piece of news that crystallized a thought.
   - **Main piece (~1,200 words):** One deep dive, either pulled from the month's Wednesday posts (expanded with context that didn't fit on LinkedIn) or written fresh.
   - **Three signals (~200 words each):** Compressed versions of the strongest Tuesday Signals from the month, each with the one-line lens that distinguishes Riché's reaction.
   - **Reading and watching (~150 words):** 3-5 pieces Riché actually engaged with this month. Cognitive science, product, AI architecture. Not a generic roundup.
   - **What I'm working on (~150 words):** One paragraph on the Context Layer Engine progress, a current product question, or a real failure he's debugging.

2. **Voice match.** Same Fatal Fifteen rules. Same proof points inventory. The newsletter is more reflective in tone (it's a longer read in a quieter inbox) but voice constraints don't relax.

3. **Subject line discipline.** Subject lines follow the same hook rules as LinkedIn opening lines: hook + specificity. "Why our AI was lying to our top user" beats "May Newsletter — Context Architecture."

4. **No "in this issue" recap.** The issue tells the reader what's in it by being good, not by telegraphing the structure.

## Examples

**Cold open example:** "A founder DM'd me Tuesday after midnight: 'Our retrieval is fine. Why isn't the agent good?' I knew the answer before I opened the message. I've sent it three times this month."

**Main piece example.** An expanded version of a Wednesday Deep Dive on the Suzy Decision Engine, with two paragraphs that didn't fit on LinkedIn (the actual sequence of meetings that produced the six-week timeline; the politics of choosing Intelligence/Insights/Impact as the three pillars).

**Reading and watching example:** "Three things I came back to this month. Klein's *Sources of Power* (recognition-primed decision making — the basis for the prioritization step in the five-step pipeline). The Anthropic blog post on context engineering (mostly right; misses the consolidation step). A 12-minute Letta talk on agent memory architecture (the closest commercial analog to what I'm building)."

## Anti-patterns
- "Recap of the month's posts." Subscribers can read LinkedIn. The newsletter must add original material.
- Newsletter as content marketing. If every issue ends in a CTA to a product or service, subscribers churn.
- Launching too early. Below ~1,500 followers, the newsletter has no audience compounding effect. Defer to Q3 2026.
