# People-First Sequencing (Horowitz, Hastings)

## What it is
People, products, profits — always in that order. Ben Horowitz's framing: take care of the people, the people take care of the product, the product takes care of the customers and the business. Reed Hastings' Netflix variant: talent density solves most other problems. If you optimize for product or profits while ignoring people, the product will degrade because the people leave or stop caring. People-first is not soft management — it is the recognition that everything else is a derivative of who's in the room.

## Why it matters
Plans are written by people. The same plan executed by a high-trust, high-density team is a different plan than one executed by a demoralized team. When you review a plan and notice friction (vague ownership, unclear handoffs, "we'll figure out who does this later"), the surface fix is "assign owners." The real fix is often a people decision — wrong person in the role, missing role entirely, or one person carrying too much.

## How to apply
1. For every plan, ask: who owns this? Not "the team" — which person, with name and judgment, is the spine? If unclear, the plan is structurally weak.
2. Watch talent density: are the strongest people on the highest-leverage work, or are they on whatever was loudest? Plans that route critical decisions to junior staff because seniors are saturated are signals to flag the staffing, not the plan.
3. Sequence morale. A plan that grinds the team for no clear reason erodes the talent that ships the next plan. Surface the trade-off explicitly.
4. Don't skip the people question to look "execution-focused." It IS execution.

## Examples
1. **Migration plan** with 6 owners, none of whom are accountable. Real fix: name one DRI, even if they don't do all the work. Without a spine, the plan won't survive contact.
2. **Quarterly roadmap** that loads every cross-functional ask onto one senior PM. Six months later that PM leaves and the roadmap dies. The flag was the load, not the roadmap.
3. **Hiring plan** that says "ramp 8 engineers in Q2." But no senior engineer is freed up to onboard them. The plan ships 8 hires and breaks the culture.

## Anti-patterns
- Saying "we're a people-first company" while building processes that route around individuals. The proof is in the staffing, not the values doc.
- Treating "team morale" as a soft metric. It is the leading indicator on every other metric in the plan.
