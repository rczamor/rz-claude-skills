# Courage Accumulation

## What it is
Confidence comes from making hard decisions, not before them. "The struggle IS the job." The 10x CEO doesn't wait to feel ready — they accept that the feeling of readiness arrives only after a decade of doing the work that scared them. Courage is not a fixed trait. It is a deposit account. Every hard decision faced and made (whether the outcome was good or bad) deposits courage. Every hard decision dodged withdraws.

## Why it matters
Plans frequently include the phrase "we'll decide later" or its cousins ("we'll figure that out in implementation," "the team will work that out"). Sometimes that's correct deferral. More often it's a courage deficit — the plan's author isn't ready to make the call and is hoping the call will become easier with time. It rarely does. The 10x CEO learns to spot the deferral that is really avoidance, and forces the call now while it is cheap.

## How to apply
1. When a plan defers a decision, ask: "Will this be easier to decide in 3 months, or are we just paying interest on the avoidance?" If the latter, force the call now.
2. Make the small hard calls. Most courage is built in tiny doses — naming the broken process, telling the underperforming hire, killing the favorite-but-failing project. These compound into the capacity to make the big calls when they arrive.
3. Accept that you will be wrong. Courage is not certainty. It is willingness to act with incomplete information and own the result.
4. Don't romanticize struggle. The struggle is the job, but the goal is to do the job, not to suffer.

## Examples
1. **Plan defers org structure**: "we'll figure out reporting lines after launch." This is almost always avoidance — it gets harder, not easier, after the launch invests everyone in the ambiguity. Force the structure now.
2. **Plan defers pricing**: "we'll set pricing in beta." Almost always avoidance. Pricing decisions made under launch-week pressure are worse than pricing decisions made now.
3. **Plan defers the firing**: an underperforming senior is on a "performance plan" that everyone knows won't work. The cowardly plan extends the timeline. The courageous plan ends the relationship cleanly, costs less, and respects everyone's time.

## Anti-patterns
- Mistaking bravado for courage. Courage is making the call that costs you something. Bravado is making the call that costs others.
- Treating each decision as starting from zero. Your past decisions inform your current capacity. Use them.
