# Wartime Awareness (Horowitz)

## What it is
Correctly diagnose peacetime vs. wartime. Peacetime habits kill wartime companies. Ben Horowitz's framing: peacetime is when the company is winning and the question is how to scale and optimize. Wartime is when the company faces existential threat and the question is how to survive. The same person, same plan, same process can be right in peacetime and fatal in wartime. The first job of the CEO is to know which mode the company is actually in.

## Why it matters
Plans inherit the assumed mode without naming it. A plan written in peacetime style during an actual wartime moment will spend resources on smooth process, broad consensus, and graceful migration — while the company is being out-shipped. A plan written in wartime style during peacetime will burn out the team and damage the culture. The 10x CEO names the mode explicitly so the plan's posture matches the actual situation.

## How to apply
1. Diagnose first. Ask: is the company winning, drifting, or losing? Is the existential threat external (competitor, market shift), internal (talent flight, culture decay), or absent?
2. Match the plan to the mode. Wartime: faster decisions, narrower scope, fewer stakeholders, blunter communication, more concentrated ownership. Peacetime: broader collaboration, more deliberation, longer time horizons.
3. Beware mode confusion. The most common failure is operating in peacetime while telling yourself it's wartime (panic without focus) or vice versa (drift without urgency).
4. Communicate the mode. The team needs to know which posture to adopt. "We're in wartime on this product line" is a real signal. Letting the team guess is a leadership failure.

## Examples
1. **Wartime plan**: incumbent ships an AI-powered version of your product. The right plan is narrow, fast, ships in 3 weeks, accepts technical debt that would be unacceptable in peacetime. Telegraphing this as "we're in wartime here" sets the team's expectations correctly.
2. **Peacetime plan**: stable product, healthy growth, plan to refactor onboarding. Right plan is collaborative, well-tested, takes a quarter. Wartime posture would damage trust and miss the opportunity to set durable foundations.
3. **Mode confusion**: company is in wartime but writes plans like peacetime. Six weeks of consensus-building while the competitor ships weekly. The plan is good; the mode is wrong.

## Anti-patterns
- Permanent wartime posture. Companies that always declare wartime burn out their best people. The mode must be earned by the actual situation.
- Refusing to name the mode. Letting the team feel a wartime weight while leadership uses peacetime language is the most demoralizing combination.
