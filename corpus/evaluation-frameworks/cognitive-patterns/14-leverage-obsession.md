# Leverage Obsession (Altman)

## What it is
Find the inputs where small effort creates massive output. Technology is the ultimate leverage — one person with the right tool can outperform a team of 100 without it. Sam Altman's framing: most plans optimize the wrong inputs. The 10x CEO obsesses over the inputs whose unit-of-effort to unit-of-output ratio is non-linear, and ruthlessly de-prioritizes everything else. Leverage is not "work smart not hard." It is selecting which work to apply hardness to.

## Why it matters
Plans accumulate work that has linear returns — work that, no matter how well it's done, produces output proportional to the effort. The 10x CEO doesn't merely want plans to be done well; they want plans to be made of high-leverage work. Adding a fourth integration when the second one wasn't pulling its weight is linear work. Replacing a manual process with an automation that runs forever is leveraged work. The discipline is asking, on every plan: "where is the leverage in this?"

## How to apply
1. For every plan, ask: "What is the highest-leverage 20% of this plan? What's the lowest-leverage 20%?" Defend the high-leverage; cut or delegate the low-leverage.
2. Look for compounding inputs: tools, automation, hires, infrastructure, distribution channels, brand. These pay back across every future plan.
3. Look for one-time inputs that unlock recurring returns: a well-designed schema, a clean API contract, a documented runbook, a teaching moment that levels up the team.
4. Avoid leverage theater: the appearance of high-leverage work that is actually just complexity. A new framework adopted to look modern is theater; a new framework adopted because it cuts ship-time in half across 5 future plans is leverage.

## Examples
1. **Internal tool**: a 1-day script that saves 30 minutes per week per engineer across 20 engineers. ~10 hours of effort, ~500 hours of return per year. Leverage.
2. **Hiring**: a senior engineer hired now reduces architectural debt across every plan for the next 3 years. The hiring effort is bounded; the return compounds.
3. **Linear work**: writing a status report that is read by 2 people and never referenced again. Linear at best, often negative-leverage.

## Anti-patterns
- Confusing busy with leveraged. Many plans have high effort and low leverage. Effort is not the metric.
- Treating every "leverage opportunity" as worth pursuing. The 10x CEO selects 1-2 high-leverage moves per quarter and protects them, rather than chasing 20 medium-leverage opportunities and finishing none.
