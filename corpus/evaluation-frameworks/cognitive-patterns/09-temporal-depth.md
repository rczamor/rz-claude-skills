# Temporal Depth (Bezos at 80)

## What it is
Think in 5-10 year arcs. Apply regret minimization for major bets. Bezos's framing: imagine yourself at 80 looking back — would you regret not taking this swing more than you'd regret taking it and failing? The 10x CEO operates in two timescales simultaneously: this quarter, and the decade. Most operators only see the quarter. The decadal frame is what makes the quarterly trade-offs legible.

## Why it matters
Plans sized only to quarterly metrics produce a portfolio of quarterly wins and decadal mediocrity. Plans sized to a 10-year vision sometimes look stupid in any given quarter — the kind of stupid that compounds into outsize outcomes. Temporal depth is what lets a CEO ship a feature this quarter that doesn't optimize the quarterly dashboard because it bends the 5-year curve. Without it, the company optimizes itself out of every long-term advantage.

## How to apply
1. For every significant plan, ask: "What does this look like in 5 years if it works? What does it look like in 5 years if we don't do it?" Both answers matter.
2. Apply regret minimization on big bets: which choice will I regret more at 80 — the swing I missed, or the failed swing? On the rare big bets, the missed swing usually loses.
3. Beware decisions that look clearly correct in a 1-quarter frame and clearly wrong in a 5-year frame. Architecture, hires, customer relationships, and product principles all sit in this category.
4. Temporal depth does NOT mean every plan needs a 5-year story. Most plans are quarterly. The discipline is knowing which plans need the long view and not flattening them into the short one.

## Examples
1. **Build vs. buy**: the buy is faster this quarter, the build compounds for 5 years. If this is the company's core surface, build. If it is plumbing, buy.
2. **Hiring senior engineer**: takes 4 months to recruit, slows the quarter. Pays back across every plan that engineer touches for 5 years. The "just hire a contractor" answer is Q1's answer; the senior hire is the decade's answer.
3. **Customer relationship choice**: discount to close a deal vs. hold the price. The discount books revenue this quarter, sets the precedent forever, and shapes the brand. The 5-year frame often picks differently than the quarterly frame.

## Anti-patterns
- Vision theater: writing 5-year visions to look strategic, then making every weekly decision against quarterly metrics. The frame must change behavior or it isn't a frame.
- Dismissing short-term plans because "they don't move the 5-year needle." Compounding requires the short-term too.
