# Narrative Coherence

## What it is
Hard decisions need clear framing. Make the "why" legible — not everyone happy. The 10x CEO knows that a decision is half the work and the explanation is the other half. People will execute against decisions they disagree with if the reasoning is honest and visible. They will quietly resist decisions they agree with if the reasoning is opaque or shifts depending on who's asking.

## Why it matters
A plan without a clear narrative is a plan that will be re-litigated every week. Every meeting, the same questions: "wait, why are we doing this again?" Without coherence, momentum bleeds out through the seams. With coherence, the plan defends itself in your absence, the team can answer "why" without reading the doc, and trade-offs feel like trade-offs instead of arbitrary cuts.

## How to apply
1. After reading a plan, write a one-paragraph narrative in your own words: what problem, what solution, what we're giving up, why this rather than the alternatives. If you cannot, the plan is not coherent yet.
2. For every controversial decision in the plan, articulate the reasoning the dissenters will hear. Not the version that wins them over — the version that lets them disagree precisely.
3. Avoid happy-talk. "This will be a win for everyone" is not narrative coherence; it is narrative avoidance. The real narrative names the cost.
4. Test the narrative by removing the conclusion. Does the reasoning still imply it? If yes, coherent. If the conclusion was bolted on, the plan is rationalization wearing a narrative.

## Examples
1. **Pricing increase**: weak narrative — "we need more revenue." Coherent narrative — "our infrastructure cost grew 40% last year, our willingness-to-pay survey shows headroom, here is the migration window we're giving existing users, and here is what we're not doing (no backdoor extraction, no surprise emails)."
2. **Feature deprecation**: weak — "low usage." Coherent — "12 active users, 3 of them on legacy contracts, 2-quarter sunset, one engineer reclaimed for the priority on the roadmap."
3. **Engineering reorg**: weak — "we're aligning to strategy." Coherent — "two teams currently both own auth, neither feels accountable, here is the new ownership, here is what each person's role becomes, here is what's NOT changing."

## Anti-patterns
- Polishing the deck instead of the reasoning. A good deck on a bad narrative looks like a great deck on a great narrative — until the first hard question.
- Confusing alignment with agreement. Coherent narrative produces alignment without requiring agreement.
