# Founder-Mode Bias (Chesky/Graham)

## What it is
Deep involvement isn't micromanagement if it expands (rather than constrains) the team's thinking. Brian Chesky and Paul Graham's framing: "manager mode" delegates fully and stays out; "founder mode" stays close to the work, asks questions, demands quality, and is willing to pull a plan apart at the seams when the seams are weak. Founder mode is exhausting and unscalable in the wrong hands. In the right hands it is the only thing keeping a company's standard from sliding into mediocrity.

## Why it matters
Plans degrade when leadership is too far away from them to see how. Layers of summary smooth out the rough edges that contain the most signal. The 10x CEO knows when to be in the weeds and when to be at altitude — and is unembarrassed about being in the weeds when the work demands it. The opposite failure mode (founder mode applied indiscriminately) crushes ownership and makes the team fragile. The skill is calibration.

## How to apply
1. Read the plan at full depth at least once. Do not let summary be your only mode of contact. The detail is where the pattern lives.
2. When you find a weak seam, ask the question that expands the team's thinking — not the question that takes over their decision. "What did you consider for X?" rather than "Do X."
3. Be in the room for the load-bearing decisions; be absent for the rest. If you are in every room, you are the bottleneck.
4. Founder-mode involvement is a privilege the team grants you with their trust. If they start hiding work from your involvement, you've lost calibration.

## Examples
1. **Plan review**: Chesky reviewing every page of an Airbnb host onboarding redesign. Not because he doesn't trust the team — because the standard he carries is the product standard. Reading the plan is how he transmits it.
2. **Wrong founder-mode**: a CEO redoing the engineer's PR comments line by line. This isn't standard-setting; this is replacing the engineer.
3. **Right founder-mode**: a CEO who reads the data flow diagram, points to one node and asks "what happens when this returns nil?" — and the team realizes they hadn't traced it. That is expanding their thinking.

## Anti-patterns
- Founder mode applied to every plan. The team learns to wait for your input rather than think. You become the constraint.
- Founder mode applied via Slack pile-ons (drive-by comments, one-line directives). High involvement without context creates noise, not signal.
