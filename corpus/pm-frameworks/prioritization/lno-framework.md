# LNO Framework

## What it is

Shreyas Doshi's three-bucket task classification: every task you take on is **Leverage (L)**, **Neutral (N)**, or **Overhead (O)**.

- **Leverage (L):** 10x+ return. Tasks where excellent execution dramatically beats average — strategy, hiring, key writing, big design decisions, customer-defining conversations.
- **Neutral (N):** 1x return. Tasks where the difference between excellent and average is small — most meetings, most operational work, routine status updates.
- **Overhead (O):** sub-1x return. Tasks where doing them well doesn't help and doing them at all may hurt — perfectionism on internal docs, over-formatting trivial Slack replies, obsessing over slide alignment for a routine review.

The discipline: apply peak energy and creative attention to L tasks. Spend the minimum on N. Eliminate or batch O.

## Why it matters

Most PMs treat all tasks as equally deserving of effort. The LNO frame breaks that — it acknowledges that effort allocation is itself a strategic decision, and that excellence is uneven by type. A PM who spends 80% of their attention on N tasks (because they fill the calendar) and 20% on L tasks is producing 80%-of-potential output. Inverting that ratio is often the highest-leverage move available, with no headcount or tooling change.

For AI products, LNO matters more. AI compresses execution time on N tasks (drafting, summarizing, scheduling). The freed time should go to L tasks — strategy, calibration, judgment — not to filling the gap with more N. Without the LNO frame, AI productivity gains evaporate into expanded N output.

## How to apply

1. **Classify every task** in your weekly plan as L, N, or O. Be honest — most tasks feel L because effort feels meaningful. Apply the 10x test: would excellent execution here actually produce 10x the outcome of average?
2. **Match energy to category.** Schedule L tasks during your highest-energy windows (morning for most). Reserve N for the after-lunch slump. Batch or delete O.
3. **Default to good-enough on N and O.** Perfectionism on N tasks is the most common time leak. The first acceptable version is the final version.
4. **Audit weekly.** End-of-week, look at what consumed the most time. If it was N or O, the ratio inverted; reset Monday.

## Examples

1. **Riché's writing decision (Suzy).** A monthly executive-level strategy memo to the CEO is L: it shapes resource allocation for the next quarter and Riché's standing with the C-suite. A weekly status email is N: stakeholders read it for 30 seconds. Spending three hours polishing the status email is O dressed as N. The memo deserves the three hours; the status email deserves twenty minutes.
2. **Pre-launch positioning at Helm Labs.** Naming the product, defining the ICP, drafting the first sales pitch — all L. Each had outsized impact on the $3.25M pipeline. Choosing CRM tooling vendor was N (any of three would have worked). Endless internal-doc formatting was O.

## Anti-patterns

- **All-L delusion.** Classifying every task as L because they all feel important. The whole point is the asymmetry — if 80% of tasks are L, the rubric isn't being applied.
- **Polishing O.** The most expensive trap: applying L-grade effort to O-grade work. Internal doc fonts, rehearsing a routine standup, agonizing over a Slack reply. The signal is internal pride; the cost is the L task displaced.
