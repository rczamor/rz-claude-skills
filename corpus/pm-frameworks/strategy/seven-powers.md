# Seven Powers (Helmer)

## What it is

Hamilton Helmer's framework (*7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy*, 2016) for the seven structural sources of durable competitive advantage. A "power" is a benefit that creates persistent differential returns — value the firm can capture that competitors cannot easily compete away.

The seven:

1. **Scale Economies.** Per-unit cost decreases as volume increases. Larger players have a structural cost advantage.
2. **Network Economies.** Value of the product to one user increases with the number of other users. Marketplaces, social platforms, communication tools.
3. **Counter-Positioning.** A new business model the incumbent cannot adopt without damaging its existing business. Disruptive entrants. (Netflix vs. Blockbuster; cloud vs. on-prem.)
4. **Switching Costs.** Customers face real cost (financial, procedural, relational) to leave. Workflow embedding, data lock-in, integration cost.
5. **Branding.** Customers attribute higher value to the product purely because of identity association. Premium pricing without functional differentiation.
6. **Cornered Resource.** Privileged access to a unique input — patent, exclusive contract, key talent.
7. **Process Power.** Embedded organizational capabilities that competitors cannot replicate even by hiring the same people. Toyota Production System, Pixar's iteration culture.

Each power has a barrier (why competitors can't replicate) and a benefit (what the firm captures from it).

## Why it matters

Most strategic claims of "competitive advantage" are not powers. They're transient — speed, brand affinity without measurable preference, "great execution." Helmer's discipline: a real power survives competitive arbitrage. If a competitor can replicate it within 12 months, it isn't a power — it's a temporary lead.

For PM strategy, Seven Powers operationalizes Biddle's "Hard-to-copy" leg of DHM. Instead of vague defensibility claims, name the specific power and explain its barrier mechanism.

For AI products, Seven Powers is especially clarifying because AI capability alone is rarely a power. Foundation models are commodities; access to them is purchasable; quality differences erode quarterly. Sustainable AI moats almost always come from scale economies (compute and data efficiency at scale), network economies (usage data improves the model for everyone), switching costs (workflow integration), counter-positioning (AI-native vs. AI-bolted-on incumbents), or process power (organizational capability to ship and improve faster).

## How to apply

1. **For each strategic initiative, name the power(s) it creates or strengthens.** No power = no durable advantage = expect competitive erosion.
2. **Identify the barrier.** Why specifically can a competitor not replicate this? Without a specific barrier, the "power" is aspirational.
3. **Identify the benefit.** What measurable economic value does the firm capture? Premium pricing, retention lift, lower CAC, lower cost-to-serve.
4. **Stress-test against time.** What does this power look like in 3 years if the competitive set responds optimally? If it erodes, it isn't a power.
5. **Pair with DHM.** DHM's "hard-to-copy" leg should always specify which of the seven powers is at work.

## Examples

1. **Suzy: switching costs + process power.** Workflow integration with stakeholder briefings creates real switching cost (changing vendors means re-aligning multiple stakeholders' templates). The methodology library refined over 350+ deployments is process power — competitors can hire the same talent but cannot replicate the iterative refinement.
2. **OpenAI vs. open-source models.** OpenAI's powers: scale economies (compute and training cost amortized across massive usage), network economies (usage data → better RLHF → better model), and brand. Open-source competitors lack the scale and network legs but counter-position on cost. The strategic question is whether scale + network grows fast enough to outpace counter-positioning's price advantage.
3. **A SaaS startup with no power.** "We have great UX." UX is replicable by any competent design team within 6 months. Not a power. Without one of the seven, the company is a price-taker once competition catches up.

## Anti-patterns

- **First-mover advantage as power.** Being first is not a power. Most first movers lose to fast followers because fast followers learn from the first mover's mistakes. Power requires a specific structural mechanism, not chronology.
- **"Culture" as power.** Culture isn't a power; the *organizational capabilities the culture produces* might be (process power). The distinction matters because cultures change with leadership; the question is whether the capabilities outlive the founder.
