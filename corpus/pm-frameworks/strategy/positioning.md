# Positioning

## What it is

Al Ries and Jack Trout's marketing-strategy concept from *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (1981). The thesis:

> **A position is a place in the customer's mind. A successful brand owns one short, distinctive idea — usually one word — that springs to mind first when the relevant category is invoked.**

Volvo owns "safety." Crest owns "cavities" (decades ago). FedEx owned "overnight." Tesla owns "electric." The position is not what the company says about itself; it is what the customer believes when prompted with a category cue.

A modern operational template (April Dunford, *Obviously Awesome*, 2019, builds on Trout/Ries):

1. **Competitive alternatives.** What would customers do if your product didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes.** What capabilities does your product have that the alternatives don't?
3. **Value (and proof).** What real value does your differentiation produce?
4. **Best-fit customers.** Who feels this value most acutely?
5. **Market category.** Where do you tell customers to shelve you mentally?

## Why it matters

Customers cannot hold dozens of brands in their head per category. The first or second name that springs to mind owns disproportionate share — purchase consideration, trust, premium pricing. Positioning is the strategic discipline of choosing which word to own and earning that ownership through consistent execution.

The most expensive strategic mistake in product is **multi-positioning** — trying to own several positions at once. Customers can't hold the multi-position; the brand becomes "something for everyone," which is mentally indistinguishable from "nothing in particular."

For AI products, positioning is acutely difficult. Every product claims to be "AI-powered." Every product claims to be the "decision engine," "copilot," or "operating system" for its category. Generic positioning doesn't position; it floats. Sharp positioning chooses a specific word to own and accepts the cost — alienating customers who don't fit the position.

## How to apply

1. **Identify the relevant category.** What mental shelf are you on? (If you don't choose, customers will choose for you — usually unfavorably.)
2. **Identify the dominant alternative.** Who or what does the customer currently use? You're competing for the same word in the same head.
3. **Choose one position.** One word. Resist multi-positioning. The position must be true (you can deliver), differentiated (no incumbent owns it), and meaningful (customers care about it).
4. **Sacrifice consistently.** Every public artifact — website, deck, conference talk, sales call, product UI — reinforces the same word. Inconsistency erodes the position.
5. **Defend the position over time.** Positions decay; competitors counter-position. Reinforce through proof points (case studies, benchmarks, customer language).

## Examples

1. **Suzy Decision Engine positioning.** Category: consumer intelligence. Position: "decision-ready" — Suzy delivers consumer evidence that's defensible enough to act on, which legacy survey tools don't. The word "decision" is anchored in the product name (Decision Engine), the pillar names (Intelligence/Insights/Impact), and the analyst-confidence framing.
2. **Riché's personal positioning (Neural Architect).** Category: AI product leadership. Position: "context architecture as strategy." Differentiated from generic AI-PM voices (who position on prompt engineering or evals); meaningful because 65% of enterprise AI failures trace to context drift. Reinforced consistently through writing, talks, and the Context Layer Engine work.
3. **Anti-pattern.** A startup positioning as "the AI-powered platform for business operations." Three positions stacked (AI, platform, business operations); none owned; nothing memorable. By contrast, a competitor positioning as "AI for AP automation" sacrifices breadth and owns a sharp position.

## Anti-patterns

- **Multi-positioning.** "We're fast and reliable and AI-powered and easy to use." Customers can hold one word per brand; multi-positioning collapses to nothing.
- **Internal-positioning.** A position that excites the team but doesn't matter to customers (e.g. "we're built on transformers"). Positions live in customer minds, not engineering decks.
