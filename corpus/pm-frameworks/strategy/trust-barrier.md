# The Trust Barrier

## What it is

Marty Cagan's concept (introduced in *Transformed*, 2024) that the largest barrier to empowering product teams isn't process, structure, or skill — it's **trust**. Specifically, the executives' trust that the team will make decisions consistent with company strategy and constraints when given the autonomy to do so.

Trust is granted, not declared. It accumulates through demonstrated judgment over time and resets quickly when violated. Most "empowerment transformations" fail not because the framework is wrong, but because executives can't trust the teams enough to grant the autonomy the framework requires.

## Why it matters

This reframes a common misdiagnosis. When empowerment transformations stall, the visible symptom is "the executives are still making product decisions." The standard prescription is more training, better OKRs, clearer principles. None of that addresses the actual cause: the executives don't trust this specific team to make this specific decision.

Trust accumulates through:
- **Outcomes delivered.** A team that consistently moves outcomes earns more autonomy.
- **Decisions defended in writing.** Showing the strategic reasoning behind decisions.
- **Surfacing risks proactively.** Not hiding bad news; raising concerns before they become crises.
- **Honoring constraints.** Operating within stated business, regulatory, and resource limits without litigating each one.

Trust collapses through:
- **Outcomes missed without explanation.**
- **Surprises** — leadership learning about a major decision after the fact.
- **Strategy deviation** without articulated reasoning.

The implication: empowerment isn't a switch. It's a graduated process where teams earn progressive autonomy through demonstrated judgment.

## How to apply

- **For PMs working under constrained autonomy:** identify the specific trust deficit. What decision would your executive sponsor not let you make today, and why? That gap is your trust-building target.
- **For executives:** state the trust criteria explicitly. "I'll trust this team to choose the solution once they've shown me three discoveries that match the strategy." Vague trust is no trust.
- **For organizations:** sequence the transformation. Don't grant full autonomy on day one; grant it in tiers as outcomes and judgment accumulate.
- **Pair with strong strategic context.** Trust without strategy is anarchy; strategy without trust is feature-team operations dressed up.

## Examples

1. **Riché at Suzy.** Early Insights pillar transition: trust-deficit was specific — leadership didn't trust the team to choose between AI-recommended-studies and methodology-coaching as the wedge. Riché closed the deficit by running a structured discovery, presenting the rationale (with discovery evidence), and committing to a measurable outcome window. Subsequent decisions came with progressively less oversight as outcomes confirmed judgment.
2. **Cagan's case study (industry).** A "transformed" company nominally empowered teams but kept executive sign-off on every major launch. The teams were not actually empowered; the trust hadn't been earned. Six months in, the talent that joined for empowerment was leaving. The diagnosis: trust gap, not framework gap.

## Anti-patterns

- **Declaring empowerment.** Announcing "this team is empowered" without addressing the trust gap. The team experiences the gap as continued executive override regardless of what's been declared.
- **Demanding trust without earning it.** PMs who frame the trust gap as a leadership failure ("they don't trust me, that's their problem") miss that trust is a two-party process. Earning it requires changes from both sides.
