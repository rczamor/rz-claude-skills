# DHM Model (Delight / Hard-to-copy / Margin-enhancing)

## What it is

Gibson Biddle's product strategy framework, expressed as a single question:

> **"How will your product delight customers in hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing ways?"**

Three sub-questions that any product strategy must answer:

- **Delight:** What specifically delights customers? Not "great UX" — name the specific delight (e.g. "personalized study recommendations that match how this analyst thinks"). Delight is measured behaviorally (retention, NPS, referral).
- **Hard-to-copy:** What makes this difficult for competitors to replicate? The answer should map to one or more of Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers: brand, network effects, economies of scale, counter-positioning, unique technology, switching costs, process power.
- **Margin-enhancing:** What about this strategy improves unit economics over time? More revenue per customer, lower cost-to-serve, increasing returns, network effects that compound.

A complete strategy answers all three. A partial strategy on any one leg fails.

From Biddle's "Ask Gib" sessions, *Reforge* materials, and his Netflix-era practice.

## Why it matters

DHM is the strategy lens because it forces the three questions every long-term product needs to answer simultaneously. Skipping any one breaks the strategy:

- **Delight without hard-to-copy:** competitors replicate within months. Your moat is months wide.
- **Hard-to-copy without delight:** customers don't care that you're defensible — they leave.
- **Delight + hard-to-copy without margin-enhancing:** you build a beloved business that loses money structurally.

Biddle's framing also reframes "innovation" — innovation isn't novelty for its own sake; it's whatever produces durable, defensible delight that the business model rewards. That's a higher bar than "we shipped something new."

For AI products, DHM is especially clarifying. AI capability alone is not hard-to-copy (every competitor has access to similar models). The hard-to-copy moat must come from elsewhere — context architecture, proprietary data, network effects on usage data, switching costs from workflow integration. The margin-enhancing question forces honest reckoning with inference-cost economics: is your unit economics getting better with scale, or worse?

## How to apply

1. **Answer the three questions** for any major strategic initiative or business unit. Write each answer in two to three sentences.
2. **Stress-test the Delight answer.** Specific behavior, not vague satisfaction. What user action increases when delight is high? Is that action measured?
3. **Map Hard-to-copy to a 7 Powers source.** If you can't name the specific power, the moat is imaginary. "We move fast" is not a power; counter-positioning, network effects, etc. are.
4. **Quantify the Margin-enhancing claim.** What unit economic metric improves? By how much, when, and through what mechanism?
5. **Sequence with GEM.** DHM is the strategy lens; GEM (Growth/Engagement/Monetization) is the prioritization lens for executing against the strategy.

## Examples

1. **Suzy Decision Engine through DHM.** Delight: enterprise analysts can answer ambiguous stakeholder questions with defensible methodology in under a day — the relief of not having to defend the methodology in a meeting. Hard-to-copy: process power (proprietary methodology library refined over 350+ enterprise deployments) plus switching costs (workflow integration with stakeholder briefings). Margin-enhancing: AI recommendations reduce per-study analyst time, lifting gross margin while increasing customer-perceived value.
2. **Netflix in 2010 (per Biddle's teaching).** Delight: personalized recommendations that surfaced content the user didn't know they wanted. Hard-to-copy: network effects on viewing data (more viewers → better recs → more viewers) and process power in content licensing. Margin-enhancing: recs reduced churn, raising LTV faster than content costs grew.

## Anti-patterns

- **Two-of-three strategy.** "Our strategy is a delightful product that's hard to copy." No margin discussion. The product becomes an expensive labor of love. Fix: force the third leg.
- **Aspiration-as-power.** "Our brand is hard to copy." Maybe — but only if that brand creates measurable preference (price premium, conversion lift, retention) at scale. Without the measurement, "brand" is wishful thinking.
