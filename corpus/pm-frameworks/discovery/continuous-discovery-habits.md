# Continuous Discovery Habits

## What it is

Teresa Torres' practice of weekly customer touchpoints conducted by the team building the product — the "product trio" of product manager, designer, and engineer — interviewing real users on a recurring cadence to discover opportunities, not validate solutions. Discovery becomes a weekly habit, not a quarterly project.

The defining shape: at least one customer interview per week, conducted by the trio together, with structured opportunity capture and assumption testing in between.

## Why it matters

Discovery as a discrete phase before delivery ("the discovery sprint") is the dominant practice and it doesn't work. By the time a quarterly discovery output reaches engineers, the underlying assumptions have aged out. Continuous discovery solves this by making the people who build the product the same people who talk to customers — every week, in perpetuity. From Torres, *Continuous Discovery Habits* (2021).

Three durable benefits: (1) the trio shares a tacit model of the customer that no research deck can replicate, (2) opportunity space stays fresh, so OSTs reflect this month's reality not last quarter's snapshot, (3) engineering judgment improves — engineers who hear customer pain build different things than engineers who read tickets.

For AI products: model capabilities evolve weekly. Customer expectations evolve weekly. A quarterly discovery cadence cannot keep up. Continuous discovery is the only cadence that compounds with the model improvement curve.

## How to apply

1. **Schedule 1+ customer interview per week, recurring.** Don't recruit on demand for specific projects — maintain a steady recruitment pipeline so the trio always has next week's interview booked.
2. **The trio attends together.** PM facilitates, designer takes structured notes, engineer probes feasibility constraints in real time. Rotate roles to keep all three engaged.
3. **Interview behavior, not preferences.** "Tell me about the last time you ran an insights study" — not "would you want a recommendations feature?" Customers can describe what they did; they cannot reliably predict what they'd want.
4. **Capture opportunities in customer language.** Direct quotes when possible. Avoid translating into internal product nouns during the interview.
5. **Update the OST after every interview.** New opportunity? Add it. Confirmation of existing opportunity? Mark its strength. Solution invalidation? Prune the branch.
6. **Pair with weekly assumption tests.** Discovery without testing produces opinions, not evidence.

## Examples

1. **Suzy Insights pillar trio.** PM, design lead, and lead engineer interview one enterprise analyst per week from their existing customer base. The standing slot is calendar-blocked for everyone; cancellations require trio approval. Six months in, the trio agrees on the customer's biggest unsolved pain without consulting notes.
2. **Helm Labs pre-launch interviews.** Riché ran weekly CFO conversations with the same five-question structure for ten weeks pre-launch. Pattern recognition across the cohort produced the positioning that drove the $3.25M pipeline — no single interview revealed it; the cumulative cadence did.

## Anti-patterns

- **Discovery research outsourced to a researcher.** When research is handed to a UX research function, the trio loses the tacit model. Researchers can scale discovery; they cannot replace the trio doing it.
- **Customers interviewed only when stakeholders demand evidence.** Discovery becomes reactive — you interview to defend a roadmap, not to learn. The cadence collapses into spurts around planning cycles.
