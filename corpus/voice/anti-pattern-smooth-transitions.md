# Avoid Overly Smooth Transitions

## What it is
Riché's voice doesn't use connective tissue between paragraphs to ease the reader from one idea to the next. The transitions are abrupt — a hard pivot, a one-line paragraph that lands the previous claim before the next one starts, or a pure full stop into a new beat. Overly smooth transitions ("This brings us to...", "Building on the previous point...", "With that in mind...") signal essay-template mode and flatten the rhythm.

## Why it matters
Smooth transitions are a tic of formal essay writing and AI-generated prose. The implicit goal is to never surprise the reader, never break the flow, never make them work for the connection. Riché's voice does the opposite. The paragraph break is the connective tissue; the reader is trusted to make the jump. When transitions are too smooth, the prose becomes wallpaper — every sentence flows into the next, and nothing lands harder than anything else. The rhythm requires friction.

## How to apply
- After drafting, read the first sentence of each paragraph. If it starts with a transition phrase ("This brings us to," "Now," "So," "Building on"), cut the phrase. The paragraph should usually start with a substantive claim.
- Hard pivots are good. A paragraph that contradicts or sharply pivots from the previous one earns the reader's attention; smoothing it loses the attention.
- One-line paragraphs are the strongest "transition" tool — they emphasize the previous claim and create white space before the next one. The white space is the transition.
- Same rule for "And so," "Of course," "Indeed," "In any case." These are connective filler.

## Examples
BAD (smooth): "Most AI teams skip the consolidation step. Building on this point, it's also worth examining how this skip affects unit economics. With that in mind, let's look at Grandstage's numbers."

GOOD (abrupt): "Most AI teams skip the consolidation step. Grandstage didn't. 300% user growth, $0 CAC. The numbers came from the upstream work."

BAD (smooth): "Context architecture is foundational. This brings us to the question of consolidation. Now, consolidation is interesting because it's where most teams fail."

GOOD (hard pivot): "Context architecture is foundational. Most teams fail at one specific step: consolidation."

BAD (smooth): "Suzy launched the Decision Engine in six weeks. Indeed, this is unusual for enterprise AI. Of course, the timeline was made possible by prior architectural work."

GOOD: "Suzy launched the Decision Engine in six weeks. That's not normal for enterprise AI. The timeline was possible because the consolidation layer was already built."

## Anti-patterns
- Replacing one smooth transition with a less-obvious smooth transition ("Furthermore" becomes "Building on this"). Same problem.
- Adding transitions because the draft "feels choppy." Choppy is often the rhythm; smoothing destroys it.
