# Story-Driven

## What it is
Story-driven is the fifth of Riché's five core voice attributes: starts with a moment, not a thesis statement. The opening of a piece is a specific scene — a customer call, an architectural decision, a number that surprised the team, a frustration that led to building something. The thesis arrives after the moment has earned the reader's attention, not before. This applies even to short LinkedIn posts and Monday Hot Takes: the lead is the specific instance, not the abstract claim.

## Why it matters
Thesis-first openings ("In today's rapidly evolving AI landscape..." / "I've been thinking about context lately...") signal generic content from sentence one. The reader's instinct is to scroll. Story-driven openings work because they trigger curiosity — what happened, what was the decision, what did they learn — and the thesis lands harder when it arrives as the resolution of the story instead of the headline. Riché's voice is calibrated to a reader who has seen a thousand AI posts and tunes out anything that pattern-matches to "AI thought leader giving a take."

## How to apply
- Open with a specific moment: a date, a metric, a conversation, a decision. Not a general observation.
- Resist the urge to state the lesson up front. Let the moment do the setup; the lesson is the closer or the middle, not the lead.
- Even Monday Hot Takes can be story-driven — one sentence of scene, then the take.
- The moment doesn't have to be long. Two lines of specific detail beat a paragraph of abstract framing.

## Examples
GOOD opening: "Six weeks. That's how long it took to ship Suzy's Decision Engine to 350+ enterprise customers. Not because the model was special. Because the consolidation layer was already built."

BAD opening: "In today's rapidly evolving enterprise AI landscape, the speed of platform transformation has become a critical differentiator."

GOOD opening: "I read a study last week that said expert decision-makers use less information, not more. I closed the tab and looked at the dashboard we'd just built at Grandstage. We were doing the opposite."

BAD opening: "It's worth noting that recent cognitive science research has implications for how we design AI products."

GOOD opening: "Helm Labs has $3.25M in pre-launch pipeline. Nothing has shipped. The pipeline exists because the context layer convinces buyers we already understand their problem."

BAD opening: "Pipeline generation is one of the most important and challenging aspects of pre-launch B2B sales."

## Anti-patterns
- Opening with the abstract claim and only later supplying the moment. The lead is the moment.
- Inventing a moment that didn't happen. The story has to be real or the voice rings hollow.
