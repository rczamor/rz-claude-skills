# Three-Domain Balance Overview

## What it is
Three-domain balance is the framework that controls topic distribution across Riché's content. Every piece reinforces at least one of three domains, with target weights: **Context Layers and AI (50%)** — "This is what I know"; **Product Management (30%)** — "This is what I do"; **Leadership (20%)** — "This is what I've earned." The strongest content sits at the intersection of two or more domains. The framework is enforced over a rolling content window, not piece-by-piece — a single Hot Take might be 100% PM, but the rolling four-week mix should track to 50/30/20.

## Why it matters
Without the framework, content drifts toward whichever domain is loudest in the moment — usually AI commentary because the news cycle is fastest there. Drift produces a feed that looks like generic AI thought leadership and fails to differentiate Riché from the cohort. The three-domain split is what makes the voice readable as a specific person: someone who knows context architecture deeply, ships PM work as a practice, and has earned leadership through specific tours (IBM, Helm Labs, Suzy, Grandstage). Holding the mix is what makes the audience trust the voice across all three.

## How to apply
- For each new piece, identify which domain it primarily reinforces. If a piece doesn't fit any of the three, the piece probably doesn't belong on the channel.
- Track the rolling four-week mix. If Context/AI has been 80% for three weeks, the next two weeks should rebalance toward PM and Leadership.
- The intersection pieces are the most valuable: a piece that uses Suzy's PM lessons to make a Context/AI claim hits 50% + 30% at once. Schedule these for high-leverage slots.
- The framework links to other skills. PM-domain content should be informed by `product-management` skill frameworks. Growth-strategy content should pull from `growth-marketing` content calendar and audience segments.

## Examples
**Context/AI piece (50%):** "Data is not context. The five-step process — curate, synthesize, consolidate, prioritize, store intelligently — is what turns retrieval into meaning. We learned this at Grandstage when 300% growth at $0 CAC came from the upstream work, not the model."

**PM piece (30%):** "Discovery is not the same as research. Discovery is the work that produces a product decision. Research is one input. Most teams I've worked with confuse the two and ship slower because of it."

**Leadership piece (20%):** "I shipped my first data platform in 2003 — Microsoft Access for Boston Beer Company. The lesson I still use: every system you build outlives the org chart that asked for it. Build for the users it will outlive."

**Intersection piece (50% + 30%):** "Suzy shipped the Decision Engine in six weeks because the consolidation layer was already built. That's a context-architecture story dressed as a PM story — the product launch was downstream of an architectural bet two years earlier. Most PMs don't sit on those bets long enough to see them pay."

**Intersection piece (50% + 20%):** "Twenty years of building data systems, and the lesson keeps showing up: the consolidation step is the one nobody wants to do. At Boston Beer in 2003, at IBM in the 2010s, at Suzy in 2024. Context is the work everyone agrees matters and nobody schedules."

## Anti-patterns
- Treating the framework as a per-piece quota rather than a rolling mix. Some pieces will be 100% one domain; the balance happens across weeks.
- Chasing the news cycle and letting Context/AI commentary creep above 70%. The drift is gradual; rebalance proactively.
