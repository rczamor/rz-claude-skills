# Editing Process

## What it is
The five-step process for editing an existing draft against Riché's voice. This is what runs when a draft already exists — written by Riché, by a collaborator, or by an AI assistant — and needs to be brought to publication-ready voice. The five steps:

1. **Check against the Fatal Fifteen first.**
2. **Check voice anti-patterns** (em dashes, structured enumeration in casual posts, jargon phrases, smooth transitions, performative closers, decorative emojis, SV-hipster register).
3. **Check terminology** (preferred vs. never-use lists).
4. **Check that there's a specific claim backed by a real proof point.**
5. **Suggest edits that sharpen, not soften.**

## Why it matters
Editing is where voice usually breaks. Well-meaning editors — human or AI — default to sanitizing, smoothing, and softening. They cut "DOPE" because it sounds unprofessional. They smooth a hard pivot into a transition. They replace a sharp Hook with a hedged claim. The process here is calibrated against those defaults. Every step is a check against the regression to generic register; the final step explicitly calls out the sharpen-not-soften rule because that's the most common edit failure.

## How to apply
1. **Fatal Fifteen first.** Run the same fifteen-rule check from the drafting process. The Fatal Fifteen catches the highest-frequency failures and should be the first pass on any draft.
2. **Voice anti-patterns.** Scan for em dashes, structured enumeration in casual posts, manufactured jargon ("retrieval-aware architectures"), overly smooth transitions, performative closers ("Been saying it."), decorative emojis, and SV-hipster register ("based," "vibes"). Cut each instance.
3. **Terminology.** Search every draft for never-use terms ("AI-powered," "leverage," "game-changing," "navigate the complexities of," "in today's rapidly evolving AI landscape," "context intelligence"). Replace with preferred terms or specific evidence.
4. **Specific claim plus real proof point.** Verify the piece contains at least one specific claim (a position the reader could disagree with) and at least one real proof point (Grandstage 300%, Suzy six weeks, etc.) connected to the claim. If either is missing, the piece needs substantive work, not just polish.
5. **Sharpen, never soften.** Every suggested edit should make a claim more specific, more direct, or more grounded — never softer, hedgier, or smoother. If an edit reduces voice intensity, reverse the edit.

## Examples
**Step 1 (Fatal Fifteen):** Draft opens with "In today's rapidly evolving AI landscape..." — rewrite from the first specific moment in the piece.

**Step 2 (voice anti-patterns):** Draft contains three em dashes — replace with full stops, colons, or commas. Draft contains a numbered list in a Hot Take — convert to prose with paragraph breaks doing structural work.

**Step 3 (terminology):** Draft uses "leverage" three times without an object — replace with use, deploy, run on. Draft uses "context intelligence" — replace with "context architecture" or "context generation."

**Step 4 (specific claim plus proof point):** Draft makes the claim "context matters" but has no proof point. Either add a Grandstage / Suzy / Helm Labs reference with the load-bearing fact, or sharpen the claim to one the writer can back.

**Step 5 (sharpen vs. soften):**
- Original: "Most AI products are doing context wrong, and I'll be honest, I'm tired of pretending otherwise."
- Bad edit (soften): "Many AI products may not optimally address contextual considerations."
- Good edit (sharpen): same as original, kept as is. The directness is the work.

- Original: "Models commoditize. Context quality doesn't."
- Bad edit (soften): "While models tend to become commoditized over time, context quality is generally more durable."
- Good edit (sharpen): same as original. The brevity is the punch.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating the Fatal Fifteen check as optional because "the draft is already good." The check is fast and catches errors that aren't visible without it.
- Suggesting an edit that softens the claim because the writer is uncomfortable with the directness. The discomfort is often the signal that the voice is working.
