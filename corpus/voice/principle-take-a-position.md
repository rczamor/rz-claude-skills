# Take a Clear Position

## What it is
Every piece must take a clear position — not a balanced survey of perspectives. The reader should be able to identify, after reading, what Riché thinks is true and what he thinks is wrong. A piece that hedges every claim, balances every position, or avoids committing on the central question fails this rule. Riché's voice is calibrated as practitioner-first and contrarian-with-substance; both of those attributes require taking a position.

## Why it matters
The default register of safe writing is the balanced survey: "On one hand X, on the other hand Y, the truth probably lies somewhere in between." That register is information-free. The reader walks away with no new claim to test against their own experience, no position to disagree with, no Hook to remember. Riché's content earns engagement because it commits. A reader who disagrees with Riché's position is engaging more than a reader who agrees with a balanced survey — disagreement is the strongest signal that a position landed.

## How to apply
- Before drafting, name the position. What does Riché think is true that other people don't? If you can't answer in one sentence, the piece doesn't have a position yet.
- One position per piece. A piece that takes three contradictory positions has no spine; even if each position is defensible, the reader can't extract the claim.
- Hedging is permitted in narrow places — a single nuance in a long Deep Dive, a "I don't know yet" in a genuine question. But hedging on the central position dilutes the piece.
- Taking a position invites disagreement. That's fine. Riché's voice can hold disagreement; it shouldn't try to preempt it.
- The position has to be supported. A position without substance is a hot take; a position with substance is an argument. Riché's voice does the latter. (See `attribute-contrarian-substance.md`.)

## Examples
GOOD (clear position): "Your AI doesn't have a model problem. It has a context problem. The teams that ship working AI products in 2026 are the ones that built the consolidation layer. The rest are upgrading the engine on a car running on the wrong fuel."

BAD (no position): "Both model improvements and context architecture are important factors in AI product success. Different teams will weight them differently depending on their specific situation."

GOOD (clear position): "Discovery is not the same as research. Discovery is the work that produces a product decision. Research is one input. Most teams I've worked with confuse the two and ship slower because of it."

BAD (no position): "Discovery and research are related concepts in product management. Some practitioners distinguish them sharply; others use the terms interchangeably. The right approach depends on team context."

GOOD (clear position): "Models commoditize. Context quality doesn't. That's the moat. Teams that invest in the model layer have an expiration date on their differentiation; teams that invest in the context layer compound."

BAD (no position): "Models are continuing to evolve, while context architecture is also a meaningful area of investment. The relative importance of each will likely depend on the specific use case and competitive landscape."

GOOD (position with one acknowledged nuance): "Most AI teams should invest in context, not model selection. The exception is teams with extreme latency constraints — at high request volumes, model choice can matter as much as context. But that's a narrow exception, not a general rule. For 90% of teams, context is the moat."

## Anti-patterns
- Adding "of course, this depends on context" hedges to make the position feel more defensible. The hedge usually weakens it.
- Surveying competing positions in lieu of taking one. The audience has access to surveys elsewhere; what they want from Riché is the position.
