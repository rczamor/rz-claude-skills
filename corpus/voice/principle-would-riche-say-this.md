# "Would Riché Actually Say This?" Test

## What it is
The final test every draft must pass before publication: would Riché actually say this out loud? This is the voice-engine's master rule. Every other rule in the corpus exists to serve this one. A draft can pass the Fatal Fifteen check, use preferred terminology, contain proof points, and still fail this test if the language doesn't match how Riché actually speaks. The test is subjective by design — it relies on accumulated familiarity with Riché's actual speech, podcast appearances, conversations, and prior writing — but it's the strongest single filter against generic register.

## Why it matters
Voice rules can be over-applied or under-applied. A draft that mechanically passes every rule (no em dashes, no "Moreover," no "game-changing") can still fail to sound like Riché if the underlying register is wrong. The "would he say this?" test catches what the rules can't fully encode — the rhythm of his speech, the way he combines warm and direct, the specific casual phrasings he uses (DOPE, pretty wild, hung up the towel) versus the ones he doesn't (no "based," no "real ones know"). The test is the safety net for everything the explicit rules miss.

## How to apply
- Read the draft aloud, in Riché's voice if possible. If a sentence makes the reader's mouth feel weird in his cadence, the sentence isn't his.
- Imagine Riché reading the draft on a podcast. Would he stumble? Would he change words mid-sentence? Would he refuse to read a phrase because it isn't how he talks? Each of those is a flag.
- The test is especially useful for pieces written by collaborators or AI assistants on Riché's behalf. They may pass every explicit rule and still fail the speech test. Restore originality by asking: how would Riché actually phrase this point in conversation?
- When in doubt, default to simpler, more direct phrasing. The rule of thumb: Riché's voice is closer to spoken language than written language. Sand-polished prose almost always fails the test.

## Examples
GOOD (passes the test): "Most AI products are doing context wrong. Honestly, I'm tired of pretending otherwise."

BAD (fails the test): "It is increasingly apparent that many AI products fail to address contextual considerations adequately." [Riché doesn't talk like a textbook.]

GOOD (passes): "We hung up the towel on the V1 architecture in week six. Pretty wild — we'd already shown the demo to investors."

BAD (fails): "We discontinued the V1 architecture in the sixth week. Notably, we had previously demonstrated the prototype to investors." [Same content, none of the texture.]

GOOD (passes): "Your AI doesn't have a model problem. It has a context problem. I'll keep saying this until people stop chasing the next release."

BAD (fails): "It is worth noting that many AI products have a context problem rather than a model problem, a finding that has implications for product strategy." [Riché doesn't write meta-commentary about his own claims.]

GOOD (passes): "DOPE book — best thing I've read on cognitive load this year. Closes the loop on why consolidation works the way it does."

BAD (fails): "An excellent book and noteworthy contribution to the literature on cognitive load. It illuminates the mechanisms underlying consolidation efficacy." [Loses the texture entirely.]

## Anti-patterns
- Treating the test as a rubber stamp once the explicit rules pass. The explicit rules and the test are independent; both must pass.
- Defending a phrase because "it's grammatically correct." Grammar is necessary, not sufficient. The voice has to sound like Riché.
