# "Expert Decision-Makers Use Less Information, Not More. Your AI Should Too."

## What it is
One of the supporting one-liners in the Hook rotation. The line connects the cognitive science finding — expert decision-makers across many fields use less information than novices, not more — to the design implications for AI products. It reframes the entire "more context" conversation. The goal isn't to give the user more data; it's to give the user the smaller, sharper, decision-ready slice that experts use. This is the most cognitive-science-grounded line in the Hook rotation.

## Why it matters
Most AI products are built on the implicit assumption that "more information helps the user." The cognitive science finding contradicts the assumption. Experts use less because they've already filtered, ranked, and prioritized; the explicit information set in their conscious decision-making is small. AI products that try to dump everything on the user are mimicking novice behavior, not expert behavior. This one-liner gives the audience a credible scientific anchor for the consolidation argument — useful in pieces aimed at builders, designers, and founders who want a citation behind the claim.

## How to apply
- Use this one-liner when the piece is making a UX argument or when the audience needs a scientific anchor to take the consolidation argument seriously.
- The two-sentence rhythm is canonical. The first sentence states the cognitive science finding; the second applies it to AI design. Both must be present.
- Strong placement: opening a Hot Take aimed at AI product designers, mid-piece in a Wednesday Deep Dive that includes one cognitive science reference (per the rule of one science insight per piece).
- Pair with Suzy's Decision Engine when possible — Suzy ships less to the user (three insights per decision, not thirty), which is a direct demonstration of the line.
- Don't pair with "Data is not context" in the same piece; both make adjacent arguments and using them together becomes repetitive.

## Examples
GOOD (Hot Take using this line): "Expert decision-makers use less information, not more. Your AI should too.

We built Suzy's Decision Engine on this principle. The user sees three insights per decision, not thirty. The consolidation layer ranks by decision relevance — what an expert in the user's role would have prioritized — and ships only that. 350+ enterprise customers, six-week launch."

GOOD (Wednesday Deep Dive using this line mid-piece): "...the cognitive science here is settled. Across chess, medicine, and emergency response, expert decision-makers consistently use less explicit information than novices. The expert has more pattern-matching internalized; the explicit working set is small. **Expert decision-makers use less information, not more. Your AI should too.** This is the design principle most AI products skip..."

GOOD (Tuesday Signal): "...new AI feature shipped this week claims to surface 'all relevant information' to the user. That's the wrong goal. Expert decision-makers use less information, not more. Your AI should too. The teams that ship fewer, sharper outputs win — see Suzy at 350+ enterprise customers and six-week launch."

BAD (truncated): "Experts use less information."

BAD (paraphrased): "Cognitive science shows that experts rely on less data than novices."

## Anti-patterns
- Using this line without connecting it to a specific design implication or built system. The line is a principle; without application it reads as theory.
- Citing the cognitive science finding without naming what the AI design implication is. The redirect from "expert behavior" to "AI design" is the load-bearing move.
