# Microsoft Access for Boston Beer Company Proof Point

## What it is
The Microsoft Access data platform Riché built for Boston Beer Company in 2003. The headline framing: **Microsoft Access data platform for Boston Beer Company (2003)**. This is the longest-arc proof point in the portfolio — the one that establishes range across more than two decades. It anchors the Leadership domain by demonstrating that Riché has been building data and context systems since well before the AI cycle, and that the consolidation pattern shows up the same way in 2003 Microsoft Access as it does in 2024 enterprise AI.

## Why it matters
This proof point is small in scope but enormous in framing. The technology is intentionally unglamorous — Microsoft Access in 2003, six SKUs across three regions, a single junior engineer who happened to talk to the operators. That's the point. The work landed because Riché paid attention to who would use the system and what decisions they needed to make. Twenty-two years later, the same instinct produces Grandstage at 300% growth and Suzy at six-week launch. The Boston Beer proof point is what makes the broader framework feel earned across cycles, not just chased.

## How to weave it in
- Use Microsoft Access / Boston Beer in Leadership-domain content, especially Friday Stories or career-arc reflections.
- Always include the year (2003). The arithmetic — twenty-plus years — is part of the proof.
- The natural framing is "I shipped my first data platform in 2003." Lead with that; let the rest unfold from the moment.
- Pair with IBM and Suzy/Grandstage when the structure is "the same lesson across three eras." The arc is the rhetorical move.
- Pull from the work for one or two specific details — six SKUs, three regions, the operators who told you what mattered. The specifics are what make the story land.

## Examples
GOOD short use: "I shipped my first data platform in 2003 — Microsoft Access for Boston Beer Company. The same instinct produces Grandstage now: pay attention to who will use the system and what decisions they need to make."

GOOD medium use (in a Leadership Hot Take): "Twenty years of building data systems and the lesson keeps showing up: the consolidation step is the one nobody wants to do. Boston Beer 2003. IBM 2010s. Suzy 2024. Different stacks, same pattern."

GOOD long use (in a Friday Story): "I shipped my first data platform in 2003 — Microsoft Access for Boston Beer Company. Six SKUs across three regions. The PM didn't exist; I was the engineer who happened to talk to the operators. We were tracking SKU velocity by region, and the operators told me which decisions they actually needed to make. Twenty-two years later, the lesson I still use: every system you build outlives the org chart that asked for it. Build for the users it will outlive."

GOOD intersection use (Leadership + Context/AI): "The consolidation step is the work everyone agrees matters and nobody schedules. I learned it on Microsoft Access in 2003. I'm still teaching it on the Context Layer Engine in 2026. The technology changes; the human pattern doesn't."

## Anti-patterns
- Citing the proof point without the year. The arithmetic is the rhetorical work; "first data platform" without "2003" is half the punch.
- Using Boston Beer for Context/AI claims that don't benefit from the long-arc framing. The proof point's value is its age; non-arc uses waste it.
