# Fatal Fifteen #14: Don't Bold Multiple Phrases Per Paragraph

## What it is
The fourteenth Fatal Fifteen rule. Bolding more than one phrase in a single paragraph is forbidden. Multiple bold phrases per paragraph is a common LinkedIn formatting tic — the writer tries to make every claim "scannable" by emphasizing key words throughout. The result is the opposite: when everything is emphasized, nothing is. The reader's eye loses the signal and the paragraph turns into visual noise.

## Why it matters
Bolding is a precision tool. It works when used sparingly — one phrase per long passage, where the bold genuinely marks the most important phrase the reader should remember. When used liberally, it signals that the writer doesn't trust the prose to carry weight on its own and is trying to compensate with formatting. Riché's voice doesn't compensate. Strong sentences carry themselves. If a paragraph needs bolding to communicate, the paragraph needs rewriting, not bolding.

## How to apply
- Audit every paragraph for bold count. If more than one phrase is bolded, cut all but the strongest one. Most often, cut all of them.
- A paragraph rarely needs bold at all. A whole post might have one or two bolded phrases — usually the canonical one-liner ("Data is not context.") — and nothing else.
- Same logic applies to italics, all-caps, and other emphasis tools. Pick one and use it once.
- The exception is genuine list structure (a numbered list with leading bold terms is fine), but lists themselves are restricted in casual posts — see Fatal Fifteen sibling rules.

## Examples
BAD: "Most teams treat **data as context** — they retrieve more, hoping more becomes meaning. **It doesn't.** The fix is **consolidation**, not **retrieval optimization**, and the **five-step process** does the work."

GOOD: "Most teams treat data as context — they retrieve more, hoping more becomes meaning. It doesn't. The fix is consolidation, not retrieval optimization. **Data is not context.**"

BAD: "We launched **Suzy's Decision Engine** in **six weeks** to **350+ enterprise customers**. The **consolidation layer** was already built; the **launch operationalized** it."

GOOD: "We launched Suzy's Decision Engine in six weeks to 350+ enterprise customers. The consolidation layer was already built; the launch operationalized it."

BAD: "Grandstage hit **300% user growth** at **$0 CAC** because the **context system did the upstream work**."

GOOD: "Grandstage hit 300% user growth at $0 CAC. The context system did the upstream work."

## Anti-patterns
- Bolding "key phrases" because the writer assumes the reader is skimming. Skimmers can find a strong sentence; weak sentences don't get rescued by bold.
- Treating bolding as a substitute for paragraph rhythm. The white space around a one-line paragraph does what bold tries to do, better.
