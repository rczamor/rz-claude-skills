# Specific Moment, Decision, Metric, or Named Tool

## What it is
The specificity rule. Every piece must contain at least one of: a specific moment (when, where, who, what), a specific decision (what was chosen, what was given up), a specific metric (a number that anchors the claim), or a specific named tool (a system, a product, an architectural component). Without specificity, the piece collapses into generic AI thought leadership. The specificity is what tells the reader the writer was actually in the room.

## Why it matters
This rule is the structural defense against generic content. The Fatal Fifteen catches surface-level voice failures; the specificity rule catches deeper substantive failures. A piece can pass every Fatal Fifteen check and still fail this rule if the substance is abstract throughout. When a piece is full of "teams," "industries," "ecosystems," "landscapes" — and contains no specific moment, decision, metric, or tool — the piece is generic at the substance layer, not just at the wording layer. Adding specificity is usually a substantive rewrite, not a polish pass.

## How to apply
- Before publishing, scan for: at least one specific moment, at least one specific decision Riché or someone Riché knows actually made, at least one specific metric (number, percentage, dollar figure, timeframe), at least one named tool (Grandstage, Suzy, Decision Engine, Context Layer Engine, etc.).
- One element is the minimum. The strongest pieces contain two or three. A short Hot Take with a single specific metric can pass; a Wednesday Deep Dive without any specifics fails.
- The metric or named tool has to connect to the claim being made. A metric that floats free of the argument doesn't earn its keep.
- If a draft has no specific moment, decision, metric, or named tool — and adding one would distort the piece — the piece probably shouldn't be on Riché's channel. The voice is calibrated against generic.

## Examples
GOOD (specific moment): "Six weeks. That's how long Suzy needed to ship the Decision Engine to 350+ enterprise customers."

GOOD (specific decision): "The hardest leadership decision I've made was killing the V1 architecture six weeks in at Grandstage. We were on track to ship something the team was proud of and the users wouldn't have used. Killing it cost morale; not killing it would have cost the company."

GOOD (specific metric): "Helm Labs is sitting on a $3.25M pre-launch pipeline at 14x ACV. Nothing has shipped."

GOOD (specific named tool): "The consolidation layer in Suzy's Decision Engine ranks by decision relevance. The user gets three insights per decision, not thirty."

GOOD (multiple specifics): "Twenty years of building data systems. Boston Beer 2003 — six SKUs across three regions, Microsoft Access. IBM 2010s — millions in revenue from personalized context systems. Suzy 2024 — six-week Decision Engine launch to 350+ enterprise customers. Different stacks. Same consolidation pattern."

BAD (no specifics): "Most teams in the AI space are missing a critical layer of the architecture. Industry leaders agree that addressing this gap is essential for the next phase of growth."

BAD (specific-sounding but vague): "We've seen significant growth at our portfolio companies through context-driven approaches." [No specific company, no specific number, no specific decision.]

## Anti-patterns
- Adding a specific number that doesn't connect to the claim. The reader notices; the specific feels grafted on.
- Treating "I've worked at companies X, Y, Z" as specificity. Brand mentions without load-bearing facts are name-dropping (see `proof-avoid-name-dropping.md`).
