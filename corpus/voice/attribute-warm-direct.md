# Warm and Direct

## What it is
Warm and direct is the fourth of Riché's five core voice attributes: first person, active voice, contractions, occasional strong language. The voice doesn't keep the reader at a clinical distance. It addresses the reader as someone in the room, uses "I" and "we" and "you," contracts naturally ("I don't," "it's," "we're"), and occasionally drops a "DOPE" or a "pretty wild" without apology. Directness shows up as short declarative sentences and unhedged claims; warmth shows up as the human texture around them.

## Why it matters
The default register of AI thought leadership is cold and corporate — passive voice, third-person framing, sanitized vocabulary, the linguistic equivalent of a press release. That register signals "this is content, not communication." Warm and direct does the opposite: it signals a person, with a perspective, talking to other people. The warmth is what makes the directness land instead of feeling combative; the directness is what makes the warmth feel like substance instead of softness.

## How to apply
- First person, always. "I built this." "We shipped this." Avoid the editorial "one might argue."
- Active voice. "We hit 300% growth." Not "300% growth was achieved."
- Contractions are natural. "I don't," "it's," "we're." Forcing the uncontracted form sounds stilted.
- Occasional strong language is fine when it sharpens. Don't sand it down because the audience might be enterprise.
- Address the reader directly — "you" — when the claim applies to them.

## Examples
GOOD: "Your AI doesn't have a model problem. It has a context problem. I've watched teams burn six months chasing the next model and still ship the same hallucinations."

BAD: "Modern AI systems often face challenges that may not be fully addressed by improvements at the model layer. Considerations around contextual data quality should be evaluated."

GOOD: "I'll be honest — when we started Grandstage, I didn't know if context-as-system would actually move the numbers. It did. 300%. And the CAC was zero, which is pretty wild."

BAD: "It is worth noting that the Grandstage initiative produced favorable user growth metrics, the magnitude of which exceeded initial projections."

## Anti-patterns
- Trading warmth for "professional" register when the audience is enterprise. They notice when the voice flattens.
- Using directness as a cover for being abrupt or dismissive. Direct without warm sounds like a corporate memo or a bad LinkedIn post.
