# Fatal Fifteen #6: No Hedging Everything

## What it is
The sixth Fatal Fifteen rule. Hedging constructions — "While it's true that... it's also important to consider...", "On one hand... on the other hand...", "It depends on context, but..." stacked across every claim — must be cut. A piece that hedges every assertion takes no position, makes no argument, and reads like a balanced survey of perspectives nobody asked for. Riché's voice takes positions, backs them with substance, and lets the reader disagree.

## Why it matters
The default register of safe corporate writing is hedging. Every claim gets a counterclaim; every assertion gets a qualifier. The result is prose that can't be wrong because it doesn't say anything. The reader feels nothing because there's nothing to feel — no spine to push back against, no claim to test against their own experience. Riché's content earns engagement because it commits. Hedged content earns scrolls.

## How to apply
- Search every draft for "While it's true," "On the other hand," "It's also important to consider," "Of course, this depends," "However, some might argue." Cut.
- One contrarian piece can hold one nuance, not five. If a piece has multiple necessary nuances, it's actually multiple pieces — split it.
- Disagreement with the writer's claim is fine. The piece is not a court ruling; it's a position. Let the reader bring their own counterclaims.
- The fix is almost always to delete the hedge and let the bare claim stand. If the bare claim feels too strong, the substance underneath it isn't actually strong enough — fix the substance, not the hedge.

## Examples
BAD: "Most teams treat data as context. While it's true that some teams have started to differentiate, it's also important to consider that the broader industry has yet to fully embrace consolidation as a discipline."

GOOD: "Most teams treat data as context. A few teams have started to differentiate. Those few are the ones with defensible products."

BAD: "Models are improving rapidly. On the other hand, context quality is also a factor that should be considered. Of course, the relative importance depends on the use case."

GOOD: "Models get smarter every month. Context quality stays the same. That gap is the work."

BAD: "Suzy launched the Decision Engine in six weeks, though it's worth noting that timelines can vary depending on team composition, prior architectural decisions, and a range of organizational factors."

GOOD: "Suzy shipped the Decision Engine in six weeks because the consolidation layer was already built. Most teams won't ship that fast because they haven't done the upstream work."

## Anti-patterns
- Replacing the hedge with a softer claim that doesn't need hedging. The fix is to commit to the original claim, not to retreat to one nobody will push back on.
- Hedging because the audience is enterprise. Enterprise readers respect a position more than they respect a survey.
