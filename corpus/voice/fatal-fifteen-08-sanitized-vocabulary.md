# Fatal Fifteen #8: Don't Sand Down the Voice

## What it is
The eighth Fatal Fifteen rule. The voice does not get sanitized for "professional" or "enterprise" register. Casual phrasing ("DOPE book," "pretty wild," "hung up the towel"), contractions, occasional strong language, and direct first-person remain in the final draft. When an editor — human or AI — softens these, the softening is the bug. Riché sounds like Riché in front of a CEO and in front of a podcast host; the register doesn't shift to flatten the personality.

## Why it matters
Sanitized vocabulary is the most common edit suggested by AI tooling and well-meaning reviewers. It's also the edit that destroys Riché's voice fastest. The texture of his content — the specific moments, the strong takes, the occasional irreverence — is what makes readers trust that a person is on the other end. Sand it down and the prose becomes interchangeable with every other AI thought leadership account. The voice is the differentiator; sanitizing the voice is sanitizing the differentiator.

## How to apply
- When reviewing a draft, ask: would Riché actually say this out loud? If the language has been smoothed into a register he doesn't speak in, restore the original.
- Casual phrasings that should stay: "DOPE book," "pretty wild," "hung up the towel," "honestly," "I'll be straight with you." These signal the human.
- Contractions stay. "I don't" not "I do not." "We're" not "we are." Forcing the uncontracted form sounds stilted and false.
- Occasional strong language is fine when it sharpens the point. It loses force if overused, but the answer is restraint, not deletion.
- The rule applies even to enterprise audiences. Enterprise readers are also humans. Sanding down on their behalf is a projection, not a need they expressed.

## Examples
BAD edit: original "Pretty wild that we hit 300% growth at zero CAC." → edited "It is noteworthy that we achieved 300% user growth with no customer acquisition cost."

GOOD: keep the original. The phrasing tells the reader Riché is also surprised, which makes the metric credible.

BAD edit: original "The book is DOPE — best thing I've read on cognitive load this year." → edited "The book is excellent and offers strong perspectives on cognitive load."

GOOD: keep "DOPE." That's the texture.

BAD edit: original "Most AI products are doing context wrong, and I'll be honest, I'm tired of pretending otherwise." → edited "Many AI products may not optimally address contextual considerations."

GOOD: keep the original. The directness is the substance.

BAD edit: original "We hung up the towel on that approach in week two." → edited "We discontinued that approach in the second week."

GOOD: keep "hung up the towel." It's specific and sounds like a person.

## Anti-patterns
- Defaulting to formal register because the audience is "important." The audience is full of people; people respond to people.
- Sanding down because a reviewer was uncomfortable. The discomfort is often the signal that the voice is working.
