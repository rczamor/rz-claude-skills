# Fatal Fifteen #11: No "Let's Dive In" / "Buckle Up" / "Here's the Thing"

## What it is
The eleventh Fatal Fifteen rule. Three specific stage-cue phrases — "Let's dive in," "Buckle up," "Here's the thing" — must not appear in any draft. They are the signature openers of the LinkedIn-influencer cohort and pattern-match instantly to manipulative engagement bait. Each one promises that something interesting is coming next; in practice the something is usually not interesting, and the cue itself has trained readers to be suspicious of the writer.

## Why it matters
These phrases are gradient signals. They suggest the writer cares more about the reader's attention than about the substance — a stage cue instead of a real claim. Riché's voice is calibrated to skip the cue and lead with the substance. If the substance is strong, the reader will keep reading without being prompted. If the substance is weak, no amount of "Buckle up" will save it.

## How to apply
- Search every draft for "Let's dive in," "Buckle up," "Here's the thing," "Here's the deal," "Listen," "Real talk." Cut.
- After cutting, the next sentence usually becomes the actual opener. If that sentence is strong, the piece is improved. If it's weak, fix that sentence — don't reintroduce the cue.
- Same rule for the LinkedIn-emoji-heavy versions: lightning bolts and rocket ships before "Buckle up" don't redeem it.
- The variants — "Strap in," "Sit tight," "Pay attention," "What I'm about to say will blow your mind" — all fall under the same rule.

## Examples
BAD: "Let's dive in. Most AI products fail because they treat data as context."

GOOD: "Most AI products fail because they treat data as context."

BAD: "Buckle up — I'm about to tell you why the consolidation layer is the moat."

GOOD: "The consolidation layer is the moat. Most teams skip it because retrieval is measurable and consolidation isn't."

BAD: "Here's the thing about Suzy's Decision Engine: we shipped it in six weeks."

GOOD: "Suzy's Decision Engine shipped in six weeks. That's the part that surprises people."

BAD: "Real talk: your AI product has a context problem."

GOOD: "Your AI doesn't have a model problem. It has a context problem."

## Anti-patterns
- Replacing "Let's dive in" with a slightly subtler variant ("Let me unpack this for you"). Same problem.
- Keeping the cue because "the audience expects it." The audience is calibrated to ignore it. Skipping the cue makes the piece stand out, not blend in.
