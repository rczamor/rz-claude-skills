# Avoid Name-Dropping

## What it is
Name-dropping is the failure mode where Riché's proof points appear in a piece without earning their place. The brand is mentioned, but the load-bearing fact (the metric, the timeframe, the architectural detail) is not. The reader sees "Grandstage" or "Suzy" and the writer expects the brand alone to do the work. It doesn't. Proof points support a claim; they are not the claim. When a proof point appears without connecting to a specific claim, it reads as a résumé flex and undercuts the rest of the voice.

## Why it matters
Riché's voice depends on practitioner-first credibility. Practitioner-first means the claim is grounded in something built — and "something built" implies a specific outcome, not just a brand association. Name-dropping inverts the relationship: the brand becomes the credential, and the claim becomes secondary. Readers who are used to LinkedIn flex content will pattern-match to that immediately and dismiss the piece. The voice is calibrated to do the opposite — the claim is primary, the proof point is the receipt, and the receipt has to be specific.

## How to apply
- Every proof point reference should include the load-bearing fact. Grandstage without "300% / $0 CAC" is name-dropping. Suzy without "six weeks / 350+ enterprise customers" is name-dropping. Helm Labs without "$3.25M pipeline / 14x ACV / pre-launch" is name-dropping.
- The load-bearing fact has to connect to the claim being made. Citing Grandstage's growth numbers in a piece about leadership culture is mismatched; the numbers don't support the leadership claim.
- One proof point reference per claim is usually correct. Repeating the same proof point three times in a short post turns the post into a brand reel.
- The test: if you remove the proof point, does the claim survive on its own? If yes, the proof point was earning its keep. If no, the claim was leaning on the brand instead of the substance — fix the claim.

## Examples
BAD (name-dropping): "I've worked at Suzy, Grandstage, IBM, and Helm Labs. The lesson is clear: context matters."

GOOD (proof points earning their place): "Grandstage hit 300% user growth at $0 CAC because the consolidation layer was already built. Suzy shipped Decision Engine in six weeks for the same reason. Different products; same architecture underneath."

BAD (brand without substance): "At Suzy, we did some great work."

GOOD (specific outcome): "At Suzy, we shipped the Decision Engine to 350+ enterprise customers in six weeks because the consolidation layer was already built."

BAD (mismatched proof point): "Leadership is about empowering teams. At Grandstage, we hit 300% user growth."

GOOD (matched proof point): "The hardest leadership decision I've made was killing the V1 architecture six weeks in at Grandstage. We were on track to ship something the team was proud of and the users wouldn't have used. Killing it cost morale; not killing it would have cost the company."

BAD (résumé reel): "I've shipped at IBM, Suzy, Grandstage, Helm Labs, and Boston Beer Company. Trust me on context architecture."

GOOD (specific claim with one proof point): "Trust me on context architecture because Grandstage hit 300% user growth at $0 CAC after the consolidation layer was added. Same pattern I've watched work for twenty years across stacks. The math doesn't change."

## Anti-patterns
- Treating the proof points as a credentials list. They aren't credentials; they're evidence for specific claims. Use them as evidence; the claim is the work.
- Citing every proof point in every piece. The proof points are valuable because they're rare and specific. Overusing them flattens that.
