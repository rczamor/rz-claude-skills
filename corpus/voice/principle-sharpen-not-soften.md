# Sharpen, Never Soften

## What it is
The editing principle that governs every suggested change. Edits to a Riché draft should make the claim more specific, the position more direct, or the prose more grounded — never softer, hedgier, or smoother. When an edit would reduce voice intensity, the edit is wrong. The rule applies whether the edit is suggested by a human, an AI assistant, or by the writer's own second-pass instinct.

## Why it matters
The default direction of editing is softening. Reviewers smooth pivots into transitions. They replace "DOPE" with "excellent." They add hedges to claims that didn't need them. They convert one-line paragraphs into explanatory blocks. Each individual softening edit feels like a polite improvement; together they erase the voice. The sharpen-not-soften rule is the structural defense — every edit must increase intensity, not decrease it. If the editor's instinct is to soften, the editor needs to recalibrate.

## How to apply
- For every suggested edit, ask: does this make the claim more specific, more direct, or more grounded? If yes, accept. If no, reject the edit and keep the original.
- Sharpening edits look like: replacing a vague claim with a specific number, replacing a hedged sentence with a committed one, replacing a smooth transition with a hard pivot, replacing a generic word with a specific one.
- Softening edits look like: replacing "DOPE" with "excellent," replacing "Most AI products fail" with "Many AI products may face challenges," replacing a one-line paragraph with a multi-sentence explanation, adding "of course, this depends on context" to a clear position.
- Special case: factual corrections are not softening. If a claim is wrong, fixing it is sharpening even if the corrected version reads less aggressively. The principle is about voice intensity, not factual cushioning.

## Examples
**Sharpen (good):**
- Original: "Most teams skip the consolidation layer."
- Sharpened: "Every team I've audited had built retrieval. None had built consolidation."

**Soften (bad):**
- Original: "Most teams skip the consolidation layer."
- Softened: "Many teams may not fully invest in consolidation as a discrete architectural step."

**Sharpen (good):**
- Original: "Suzy launched the Decision Engine in six weeks."
- Sharpened: "Six weeks. Suzy shipped the Decision Engine to 350+ enterprise customers in six weeks because the consolidation layer was already built."

**Soften (bad):**
- Original: "Suzy launched the Decision Engine in six weeks."
- Softened: "Suzy launched the Decision Engine in approximately six weeks, though timelines can vary depending on team composition."

**Sharpen (good):**
- Original: "Models commoditize. Context quality doesn't."
- Sharpened (kept as is, sometimes the original is already sharpened): "Models commoditize. Context quality doesn't. That's the moat."

**Soften (bad):**
- Original: "Models commoditize. Context quality doesn't."
- Softened: "While models tend to become commoditized over time, context quality is generally more durable as a differentiator."

**Sharpen (good):**
- Original: "Honestly, the consolidation step is the difference."
- Sharpened (kept): same. The "honestly" is voice; the directness is the work.

**Soften (bad):**
- Original: "Honestly, the consolidation step is the difference."
- Softened: "It is worth noting that consolidation may serve as a meaningful differentiator."

## Anti-patterns
- Treating sharpening and politeness as opposites. Riché's voice is warm and direct simultaneously; sharpening doesn't have to feel cold.
- Defending a softening edit because the audience is "professional." Professional audiences are also human; sharpened prose lands more credibly than sanded prose.
