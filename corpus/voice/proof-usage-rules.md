# Proof Point Usage Rules

## What it is
The operating rules for when and how to weave proof points (Grandstage, Helm Labs, IBM, Suzy, Context Layer Engine, Microsoft Access for Boston Beer Company) into Riché's content. The proof points are the receipts that back the practitioner-first voice; using them well is one of the strongest signals that a piece is genuine. Using them badly — name-dropping, mismatching the proof point to the claim, citing without specifics — undercuts the rest of the voice.

## Why it matters
A proof point is load-bearing only when it's specific, on-domain, and supports the claim it's attached to. A piece that mentions "Suzy" without the six-week launch number, the 350+ enterprise customer scale, or the consolidation-layer architectural detail is just name-dropping. A piece that uses Grandstage's growth numbers to support a Leadership claim about org culture is mismatched — the proof point belongs to a different domain and the audience can tell. These usage rules prevent the most common failures.

## How to apply
1. **Match the proof point to the claim's domain.** Context/AI claims pull from Grandstage, Context Layer Engine, and the Context/AI angles of Suzy and IBM. PM claims pull from Suzy, Helm Labs, and the PM angles of Grandstage. Leadership claims pull from Boston Beer 2003, IBM tours, and career-arc framings.
2. **Lead with the specific number or detail.** Every proof point has a load-bearing fact: 300% / $0 CAC for Grandstage, $3.25M / 14x ACV for Helm Labs, 350+ / six-week for Suzy, 2003 / six SKUs / three regions for Boston Beer. Use the fact, not the brand.
3. **Limit to one or two proof points per piece.** A Hot Take uses one; a Wednesday Deep Dive can use two or three; rarely four. Stacking too many proof points reads as résumé performance.
4. **Pair proof points across eras when the claim is about the longevity of the framework.** Boston Beer (2003) + IBM (2010s) + Suzy (2024) is the arc; one element of the arc is fine but the trio is more powerful.
5. **Honor the qualifiers.** Helm Labs is "pre-launch pipeline only, nothing shipped." Context Layer Engine is "in development." These qualifiers are non-negotiable; skipping them is dishonest.

## Examples
GOOD (specific, on-domain, single proof point): "Grandstage hit 300% user growth at $0 CAC because we treated context as a system, not a side effect. The model wasn't the differentiator."

BAD (name-dropping without specifics): "At Grandstage, we built a context system. At Suzy, we shipped a Decision Engine. At Helm Labs, we built a pre-launch context layer."

GOOD (paired across eras for longevity claim): "Twenty years of building data systems. Boston Beer 2003 — six SKUs across three regions, Microsoft Access. IBM 2010s — millions in revenue from personalized context systems. Suzy 2024 — six-week Decision Engine launch to 350+ enterprise customers. Different stacks. Same consolidation pattern."

BAD (mismatched domain): "Suzy launched Decision Engine in six weeks, which is what great leadership looks like." [Suzy is a PM/Context-AI proof point; mismatching to Leadership claim feels forced.]

GOOD (intersection, two proof points, two domains): "Two proof points for the context-as-moat thesis: Grandstage (300% / $0 CAC, post-launch) and Helm Labs ($3.25M pipeline / 14x ACV, pre-launch). Different lifecycle stages. Same architecture underneath."

## Anti-patterns
- Citing a proof point as a credibility flex without connecting it to the claim. The claim has to use the proof point; the proof point doesn't credential the writer.
- Stacking three to five proof points in a single short post. One per Hot Take is enough; more reads as performance.
