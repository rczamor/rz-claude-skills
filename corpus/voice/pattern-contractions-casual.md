# Contractions and Casual Phrasing

## What it is
Contractions are natural in Riché's voice — "I don't," "it's," "we're," "that's," "won't." Casual phrasings show up too: "DOPE book," "pretty wild," "hung up the towel," "honestly," "I'll be straight with you." This pattern is what makes the voice sound like a person talking instead of an institutional press release. The contractions and casual phrasings carry warmth; they're the texture that makes the directness land instead of feeling cold.

## Why it matters
Uncontracted prose ("I do not believe that..." / "It is important to note that...") signals corporate or AI-generated register. Even one or two unnatural uncontracted forms in a piece can shift the reader's perception of the writer. Casual phrasings work the same way in reverse — they signal a human author with personality. "DOPE book" tells the reader Riché actually liked the book and wants you to know it. "Hung up the towel" tells the reader he's recalling a real moment, not generating a polished anecdote. The pattern is non-negotiable; sanding it down (see Fatal Fifteen #8) destroys the voice.

## How to apply
- Use contractions everywhere they would naturally occur in speech. "I don't," "it's," "we're," "didn't," "won't."
- The exception is when the uncontracted form is genuinely emphatic ("I do not agree" carrying real weight). Use sparingly.
- Casual phrasings should be authentic to Riché's actual speech — "DOPE," "pretty wild," "hung up the towel," "honestly." These are real to him; manufactured casual phrasings ("hot take," "real talk") read as borrowed.
- The casual phrasings appear sparingly across a piece. Two or three in a long post; one in a Hot Take; sometimes none at all. They're seasoning, not the main ingredient.
- When an editor (human or AI) un-contracts the prose or replaces "DOPE" with "excellent," restore the original.

## Examples
GOOD: "I don't think the model is the problem. It's the context."

BAD: "I do not believe the model is the problem. It is the context."

GOOD: "We're building the consolidation layer most teams skip. Honestly, that's the moat."

BAD: "We are building the consolidation layer that most teams skip. It is, in our view, the competitive moat."

GOOD: "Read this book over the weekend — DOPE. Best thing I've read on cognitive load this year."

BAD: "Read this book over the weekend. It is excellent and offers strong perspectives on cognitive load."

GOOD: "We hung up the towel on that approach in week two. The retrieval layer was working, but consolidation was nowhere."

BAD: "We discontinued that approach in the second week. While the retrieval layer was functional, the consolidation component was insufficient."

GOOD: "Pretty wild that Grandstage hit 300% growth at zero CAC. I didn't fully believe the number until I traced the funnel."

BAD: "It is remarkable that Grandstage achieved 300% growth with no customer acquisition cost. I did not initially have full confidence in the figure until I had performed funnel analysis."

## Anti-patterns
- Forcing casual phrasings in every paragraph. They lose effect with overuse; sprinkle them.
- Replacing "DOPE" or "pretty wild" with "remarkable" or "noteworthy" when editing for an enterprise audience. The audience is full of humans; the casual phrasing reads more credibly than the corporate one.
