# No Generic AI Thought-Leader Voice

## What it is
The master test for AI-generated and AI-assisted drafts: if a piece could have been written by any AI thought leader, rewrite. The test isn't about whether an LLM technically produced the text; it's about whether the substance, voice, and specifics are unique to Riché. A piece that hits all the surface markers (no Fatal Fifteen failures, preferred terminology, decent rhythm) but contains no specifics that distinguish it from generic AI commentary fails this principle and must be rewritten.

## Why it matters
The AI thought leadership cohort is enormous. Most of the content in the cohort is interchangeable — it pattern-matches to a "smart take on AI" register that any LLM can produce convincingly. Readers have been pattern-trained to scroll past it. Riché's voice has to actively differentiate from the cohort, and the differentiation is structural: specific moments, real proof points, contrarian positions backed by built systems, warm-direct prose that sounds like a person. When a piece lacks this structural differentiation, the surface polish doesn't save it.

## How to apply
- After drafting, read the piece as if a stranger wrote it. Could it have been posted by any AI thought leader on LinkedIn this week? If yes, the piece fails the test.
- The most common failure modes: generic claims about "AI," "the industry," "teams," "leaders" without naming specifics; clean-but-empty rhythm (variable paragraph length but no built-system proof points); positions that sound bold but resolve to consensus claims.
- The fix is almost always substantive, not surface-level. Adding a Grandstage / Suzy / Helm Labs proof point with a load-bearing fact. Replacing a generic claim with a specific moment. Sharpening a hedged position into a committed one.
- A useful counter-test: would Riché's competitors have written this? If yes, the piece doesn't differentiate. If no, the piece is doing voice work.

## Examples
BAD (generic, fails the test): "AI is changing how teams build products. The most successful organizations are the ones that adapt their architecture to leverage these emerging capabilities. Context will play an increasingly important role in differentiating winners from losers."

GOOD (specific, passes the test): "Most AI teams treat data as context. They retrieve more, hoping more becomes meaning. It doesn't. We learned this at Grandstage when 300% user growth at $0 CAC came from the consolidation layer, not the model. The fix isn't a better embedding. It's the unglamorous middle step most teams skip."

BAD (generic, sounds bold but isn't): "Bold take: not every AI feature deserves to ship. Teams need to prioritize ruthlessly to win in this market."

GOOD (specific, actually contrarian): "If your roadmap doesn't have a deprioritized list, it's not a roadmap. It's a wish list. We killed three features at Suzy in the six weeks before Decision Engine shipped because they would have soaked focus the launch needed. 350+ enterprise customers got the launch on day one because we said no to the smaller stuff publicly enough that the team believed we meant it."

BAD (generic, decent rhythm but no specifics): "Most AI products fail.

The reason isn't the model.

It's the architecture underneath.

Teams that win in 2026 are the ones that invest in the right layers."

GOOD (specific, real rhythm + substance): "Most AI products fail.

The reason isn't the model. It's the consolidation layer the team skipped.

We learned this at Grandstage. 300% user growth at $0 CAC came from adding consolidation downstream of retrieval — same data, better context. The model never changed."

## Anti-patterns
- Treating the principle as a polish step. It's a structural test; failing it usually requires substantive rewriting, not surface edits.
- Trusting "this is well-written" as a sign the principle passed. Generic AI prose is well-written by design. Well-written and generic are not opposites.
