# Authoritative but Accessible

## What it is
Authoritative but accessible is the first of Riché's five core voice attributes. The content speaks from earned credibility — built systems, measured outcomes, real architectural decisions — but never hides behind jargon, abstraction, or academic register. The reader should feel that the writer knows more than they're saying, but is still translating into language that lands without a glossary.

## Why it matters
The failure mode this attribute prevents is the "AI thought leader" register: confident-sounding but generic, hedged behind buzzwords ("retrieval-aware architectures," "context intelligence"), or academic in a way that signals credentials without conveying meaning. Authority without accessibility reads as posturing. Accessibility without authority reads as fluff. Riché's voice has to do both at once because his readers are sharp practitioners who can tell when a claim has been earned versus performed.

## How to apply
- Lead with a concrete observation, decision, or outcome. Save the abstraction for the second beat.
- If a sentence requires the reader to know a niche academic term to parse, rewrite it. Either define the term in context or pick a plainer phrasing.
- Test every paragraph: would a smart product person who hasn't read information theory follow this? Would an information theorist still respect it?
- Strong language is allowed when it sharpens the point. Sanded-down vocabulary signals the writer is afraid to be wrong.

## Examples
GOOD: "Grandstage hit 300% user growth at $0 CAC because we treated context as a system, not a side effect of the product. Every ICP got a curated, consolidated view by the time they opened the app."

BAD: "Through the strategic deployment of retrieval-aware architectures and context-intelligent paradigms, Grandstage achieved unprecedented growth metrics."

GOOD: "Suzy's Decision Engine launched in six weeks because the consolidation step was already designed. We didn't have to invent context generation; we had to operationalize it for 350+ enterprise customers."

BAD: "Suzy's Decision Engine launch represented a paradigm shift in how enterprise customers leverage AI-powered insights."

## Anti-patterns
- Using technical vocabulary as a credibility signal rather than a precision tool. If the term isn't doing work, cut it.
- Switching to academic register when the reader pushes back. Sharpen the claim instead of insulating it with hedges.
