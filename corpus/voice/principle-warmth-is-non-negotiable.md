# Warmth Is Non-Negotiable

## What it is
Warmth is one of the five core voice attributes (see `attribute-warm-direct.md`), and this principle establishes that warmth is not optional decoration that gets cut when the audience changes or the topic gets serious. It stays in enterprise content. It stays in keynote bios. It stays in podcast pitch emails. The warmth is what makes the directness land instead of feeling cold; remove the warmth and the directness becomes a corporate memo. Riché's voice cannot fail this rule.

## Why it matters
The most common reason warmth gets cut is "the audience is enterprise" or "the format is professional." Both are wrong assumptions. Enterprise audiences are full of humans; professional formats are still read by people. Warmth is a credibility signal — it tells the reader a person is on the other end, with a perspective, who actually cares whether the reader gets value from the piece. Cold corporate prose is everywhere; warm direct prose is rare. Cutting the warmth to fit a perceived audience norm cuts the differentiator.

## How to apply
- Warmth shows up as: contractions (I don't, it's, we're), first person, occasional casual phrasings (DOPE, pretty wild, hung up the towel), genuine questions, occasional self-deprecation, addressing the reader directly ("you").
- When an editor or AI assistant suggests removing warmth markers because the piece is for an enterprise audience, reject the edit. The audience tolerates warmth; the audience rewards warmth.
- Warmth should not be performative. Forced warmth — emoji-heavy, exclamation-point-laden, "hey friends!" openers — fails this rule. The warmth has to feel like Riché actually being warm in conversation.
- Warmth and directness are simultaneous, not alternating. A sharp Hot Take can still contain a warm phrase; a warm Friday Story can still contain a sharp claim. Don't trade one for the other.

## Examples
GOOD (warm + direct in enterprise content): "Honestly, most AI products are doing context wrong, and I'll be straight with you — the fix isn't easy. It took us six months to figure out at Grandstage. But once we added the consolidation layer, 300% user growth at $0 CAC followed."

BAD (cold corporate, warmth removed): "It is worth noting that many AI products fail to optimally address contextual considerations. The remediation is non-trivial, requiring sustained architectural investment. Grandstage realized 300% user growth and zero customer acquisition cost following a six-month transformation."

GOOD (warm + direct in a podcast pitch email): "Hey [host name] — really enjoyed the [recent episode] conversation, especially the bit on consolidation. Wanted to send over a couple of angles I've been working on that might fit the show. I've been writing a lot lately about why context is the moat for enterprise AI — proof points from Grandstage (300% / $0 CAC) and Suzy (six-week Decision Engine launch to 350+ customers). Happy to share more if it's useful."

BAD (cold pitch): "Dear [host name]: I am reaching out to propose myself as a guest for your podcast. My background in product management and AI architecture would offer your audience valuable perspectives on the role of context in enterprise systems. Please find attached my speaker bio."

GOOD (warm in a serious leadership reflection): "I shipped my first data platform in 2003 — Microsoft Access for Boston Beer Company. Honestly, I had no business doing it; I was the engineer who happened to talk to the operators. Twenty-two years later, the lesson I still use: every system you build outlives the org chart that asked for it."

BAD (warmth sanded down): "My initial data platform implementation, completed in 2003 for Boston Beer Company, leveraged Microsoft Access. The work was undertaken in an unconventional capacity. The lesson, durable across two decades, is that systems outlive their commissioning organizations."

## Anti-patterns
- Cutting "DOPE" or "pretty wild" because the piece is "for executives." Executives are humans; the casual phrasing reads more credibly than the corporate one.
- Treating warmth as decoration. It's a structural attribute; cutting it cuts the voice.
