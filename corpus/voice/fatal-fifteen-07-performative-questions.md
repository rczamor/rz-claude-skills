# Fatal Fifteen #7: No Performative Rhetorical Questions

## What it is
The seventh Fatal Fifteen rule. Performative rhetorical questions — "But what does this mean for the future of work?", "Are we ready for what comes next?", "Where do we go from here?" — must not appear in any draft. These questions don't seek answers; they pretend to invite reflection while actually filling space and stalling before another generic claim. They are the verbal equivalent of a dramatic pause where nothing dramatic happens.

## Why it matters
Every reader has been pattern-trained to skip past performative questions. They appear in every TEDx talk, every LinkedIn essay, every conference keynote. The reader knows the writer isn't actually asking — and the gap between performance and substance is what makes the piece feel hollow. Riché's voice uses questions, but only when they come from real curiosity or real frustration. The question has to be one Riché would actually want answered, not a stage cue.

## How to apply
- Search every draft for question marks. For each one, ask: would Riché actually want an answer to this? If no, cut.
- Real questions in Riché's voice often start with "Why," "What's underneath," or "What if" — and they show up in the middle of pieces, not as section transitions or closers.
- Performative questions almost always pair with a generic claim ("But what does this mean for the future of work? Everything."). The pair is the giveaway.
- If you've cut the question and the surrounding paragraph still works, the question was performative. If it doesn't work, the question was load-bearing — keep it but make it specific.

## Examples
BAD: "But what does this mean for the future of AI products? It means everything."

GOOD: "Most AI products fail at the consolidation step. The retrieval works. The synthesis works. Then the system dumps everything on the user and calls it context."

BAD: "Are we ready for what comes next in enterprise AI?"

GOOD: "Suzy shipped the Decision Engine in six weeks. Most enterprise teams aren't ready to move that fast because they haven't done the upstream work on consolidation."

BAD: "Where do we go from here? The answer lies in context."

GOOD: "The answer is consolidation. Curate, synthesize, consolidate, prioritize, store intelligently. The five steps are the work."

GOOD genuine question (in middle of piece): "Why do most AI teams skip the consolidation step? My guess: because retrieval is measurable and consolidation isn't. We optimize what we can grade."

## Anti-patterns
- Replacing one performative question with a slightly fancier performative question. Same problem.
- Using performative questions as section breaks. They don't earn the break; the substance earns the break.
