# Fatal Fifteen #10: Extend, Don't Summarize

## What it is
The tenth Fatal Fifteen rule. When referencing other content — a study, an article, a competitor's product launch, somebody else's post — extend or challenge the source. Never summarize it. A summary post adds nothing to the conversation and signals the writer has no take. Tuesday Signals especially live or die by this rule: react to specific news through the context generation lens, but always extend or challenge.

## Why it matters
The internet has infinite summarizers. The premium is on people who add a frame, a contradiction, or an extension that the source itself didn't make. When Riché posts about somebody else's content, his job is to put the source through his lens — context generation, practitioner-first, the three-domain balance — and produce something the source's author wouldn't have written. Summary content is interchangeable; extension content is unique to the writer's perspective.

## How to apply
- Before writing about a source, name the angle. What's the take Riché brings that the original author didn't? If there isn't one, don't post.
- The lens is usually one of: context generation reframe, practitioner pushback ("here's why this won't work in production"), framework extension ("the source covers steps 1-3; here's what 4-5 look like"), or contradiction with evidence.
- If the post starts with a recap of the source, cut the recap. Open with the take; the source becomes the second beat or the example.
- Quote the source briefly to anchor the reader, then immediately pivot to the extension.

## Examples
BAD (summary): "Anthropic published a great piece on context engineering this week. They argue that context is more important than the model, and walk through several examples of how teams are getting it wrong. Worth a read."

GOOD (extension): "Anthropic's piece on context engineering this week is doing the right work, and it's still missing the consolidation layer. Curate-synthesize-retrieve gets most teams 70% of the way. The remaining 30% — turning retrieved data into decision-ready context — is where production AI products actually fail. We learned this the hard way at Suzy. The Decision Engine ships less to the user, not more."

BAD (summary): "OpenAI announced GPT-5 today with significant improvements across reasoning, coding, and multimodal benchmarks. The new model is faster and cheaper than the previous generation."

GOOD (challenge): "GPT-5 dropped today. Faster, cheaper, smarter. The teams that benefit are the ones who already fixed their context layer. Everyone else is upgrading the engine on a car with the wrong fuel. Models commoditize. Context quality doesn't. That gap is the moat."

## Anti-patterns
- Quoting the source at length to fill the post, then adding a one-line take at the end. The proportions are inverted. The take should be the post; the source should be the receipt.
- Extending without naming the source. Credit the source; the extension still works and the integrity stays intact.
