# Leadership (20%)

## What it is
The Leadership domain is the smallest share of Riché's content — 20% of the rolling mix. The frame is "This is what I've earned." Content in this domain comes from twenty-plus years of building, leading teams, navigating org change, mentoring product builders, and reflecting on what worked and what didn't. Friday Stories often live here. The proof points span the full career — Microsoft Access for Boston Beer Company in 2003, the IBM tours, Suzy's six-week platform transformation, the Grandstage rebuild, and the lessons Riché teaches in office hours.

## Why it matters
The Leadership share is what gives the voice depth across time. Without it, the content reads as if Riché only started building when AI got hot. With it, the content reads as someone who has shipped through multiple cycles — the Microsoft Access era, the data platform era, the SaaS era, the AI era — and has earned the framing he uses now. The 20% share is small on purpose; leadership content is most valuable when it's rare. A reader who sees a Friday Story about the Boston Beer Company project remembers it because it's not the every-week pattern.

## How to apply
- Roughly 20% of the rolling four-week content mix should center on leadership — career arc, lessons across tours, mentorship moments, reflective stories that distill into a principle.
- Lead with the specific moment. Leadership content fails when it generalizes ("I've learned that great leaders..."). It works when it stays in a specific room, a specific decision, a specific year.
- Friday Stories are the natural format. Chronological narrative; honest, warm; ends on a distilled lesson, not a lecture. The story does most of the work.
- The proof points span the full career — Boston Beer 2003, IBM, Helm Labs, Suzy, Grandstage. Use older proof points sparingly but deliberately. They establish range.
- Leadership content can also intersect Context/AI. The point that "the consolidation step is the one nobody wants to do" is a Leadership claim about how teams behave, not a Context/AI architectural claim.

## Examples
GOOD Leadership Friday Story: "I shipped my first data platform in 2003 — Microsoft Access for Boston Beer Company. Six SKUs across three regions. The PM didn't exist; I was the engineer who happened to talk to the operators. Twenty-two years later, the lesson I still use: every system you build outlives the org chart that asked for it. Build for the users it will outlive."

GOOD Leadership Hot Take: "The hardest part of leadership isn't choosing the right thing. It's killing the wrong thing publicly enough that the team believes you mean it next time."

GOOD Leadership Wednesday Deep Dive (rare format for this domain): A 900-word reflection on the Suzy six-week platform transformation — the decisions, the team conversations, the moments of doubt, what got cut to make room. Specific, narrative, ends on a single distilled lesson.

GOOD intersection piece (Leadership + Context/AI): "Twenty years of building data systems, and the lesson keeps showing up: the consolidation step is the one nobody wants to do. At Boston Beer in 2003, at IBM in the 2010s, at Suzy in 2024. The technology changes; the human pattern doesn't. Context is the work everyone agrees matters and nobody schedules."

GOOD Leadership Thursday Framework (rare): The five questions Riché asks himself before saying yes to a leadership role, with one example of a yes and one example of a no.

## Anti-patterns
- Generic leadership advice ("Great leaders empower their teams"). The voice has no room for it; leadership content has to come from a specific room.
- Letting Leadership share drop to 5% because the AI news cycle is loud. The Leadership share is the depth signal; cutting it shrinks the voice.
