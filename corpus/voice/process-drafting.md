# Drafting Process

## What it is
The five-step process for producing a new draft in Riché's voice. The process front-loads voice calibration so the draft enters review already passing the voice rules, rather than relying on a heavy edit pass to fix systemic register problems. The five steps:

1. **Identify content type** — hot take, signal, deep dive, framework, story (unless obvious from the brief or context).
2. **Write the first draft in Riché's voice** using the patterns above (variable rhythm, contractions, specific moments, story-driven openings).
3. **Self-check against the Fatal Fifteen** before presenting.
4. **AI-thought-leader test** — if the draft could have been written by any AI thought leader, rewrite.
5. **Specificity test** — does it contain a specific moment, decision, metric, or named tool? Does it take a clear position? Could Riché say this out loud?

## Why it matters
A drafting process that catches voice problems before they're written produces fewer review cycles and stronger pieces. The Fatal Fifteen and never-use terminology lists are easy to apply during writing if the writer is paying attention; they're harder to retrofit through editing because the underlying structure of the piece may have been built around the voice failure. Front-loading the voice calibration means the draft has the right bones from the start.

## How to apply
1. **Identify content type.** A Monday Hot Take has different rhythm and length constraints than a Wednesday Deep Dive. The format affects sentence length, paragraph rhythm, and the proportion of proof points to commentary. Establish the type before drafting; if unclear, ask Riché.
2. **Write the first draft in voice.** Use the patterns explicitly: variable rhythm, contractions, casual phrasings where they fit, story-driven openings. Don't draft a "neutral" version and plan to add voice later — voice has to be in the bones of the draft.
3. **Self-check against the Fatal Fifteen.** Search for each of the fifteen patterns ("In today's...", filler transitions, symmetrical triples, empty superlatives, faux-profound closings, hedging, performative questions, sanitized vocabulary, uniform paragraph length, summarizing instead of extending, "Let's dive in/Buckle up/Here's the thing", thrilled/excited/honored, navigate metaphor, multi-phrase bolding, Key Takeaway blocks). Rewrite any section that contains them.
4. **AI-thought-leader test.** Read the draft as if a stranger wrote it. Could it have been written by any LinkedIn AI commentator? If yes, rewrite — the substance probably needs more specific moments, more proof points, and a sharper position.
5. **Specificity test.** Verify the draft contains: a specific moment (when, where, who, what), a specific metric or named tool (Grandstage 300%, Suzy six weeks, etc.), a clear position the reader could disagree with, and prose Riché could say out loud without sounding like a press release.

## Examples
**Step 1 (identify):** A draft for "the new model release" likely needs to be a Tuesday Signal — analytical, additive, 200-400 words, react to specific news through the context generation lens.

**Step 2 (draft in voice):** Open with a story moment (the news event). Use variable rhythm. Use a Hook one-liner that fits the angle. Reference one or two proof points — Grandstage post-launch, Suzy six-week — to demonstrate context-as-moat.

**Step 3 (Fatal Fifteen check):** Scan for openers ("In today's..."), transitions ("Moreover..."), symmetrical triples, etc. Rewrite as needed.

**Step 4 (AI-thought-leader test):** If the piece reads like generic model-release commentary, rewrite. The differentiator is the context-architecture frame and the specific proof points — both must be present and load-bearing.

**Step 5 (specificity check):** The piece names the model release, contains the Hook ("Your AI doesn't have a model problem. It has a context problem."), references the Grandstage 300% / $0 CAC outcome, and takes the position that context architecture is the durable moat. Position is clear; reader can disagree. Riché could say it on a podcast without flinching.

## Anti-patterns
- Skipping the content-type identification and producing a piece that doesn't fit any format. The format constraints exist for reasons; ignoring them produces shapeless drafts.
- Treating the AI-thought-leader test as a formatting check rather than a structural one. If the draft fails, the rewrite is usually substantive — more specificity, sharper position, real proof point — not surface-level edits.
