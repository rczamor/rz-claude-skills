# Genuine Questions

## What it is
Questions in Riché's voice come from real curiosity or real frustration. They're never performative. A genuine question is one Riché would actually want answered if a sharp practitioner were sitting across from him — "Why do most AI teams skip consolidation?", "What if the model layer commoditizes faster than people expect?", "Why is retrieval so much easier to build than consolidation?". The reader recognizes the difference between a real question and a stage cue, and the voice is calibrated to use only the former.

## Why it matters
Performative questions ("But what does this mean for the future of work?") have been so over-deployed in thought-leadership prose that readers automatically discount them. They signal the writer is filling space and stalling. Genuine questions do the opposite — they signal the writer has actually been thinking about the problem and hasn't fully resolved it. That uncertainty is part of the appeal. It makes the writer seem like a peer, not a guru. Riché's voice cultivates that peer relationship; genuine questions are one of the strongest tools for it.

## How to apply
- Before including a question in a draft, ask: would Riché actually want an answer to this? If yes, keep. If no, cut.
- Real questions tend to start with "Why," "What if," or "What's underneath." They tend to be specific to the problem at hand, not gestures at a vague theme.
- Real questions appear in the middle of pieces more than at the start or end. They mark the place where Riché's thinking is actually open.
- It's fine to leave a real question unanswered. Closing a piece with an honest "I don't know yet" is stronger than closing with a fake answer.
- One genuine question per piece is usually enough. Multiple questions in a row can feel performative even when each one is real.

## Examples
GOOD (genuine, in middle of piece): "Why do most AI teams skip the consolidation step? My guess is that retrieval is measurable and consolidation isn't. We optimize what we can grade. But I'm not fully convinced — there might be something more structural going on."

BAD (performative): "But what does this mean for the future of AI products? It means everything."

GOOD (genuine, leaving open): "What if the model layer commoditizes faster than people expect? I don't know. The teams I've worked with have been planning for a 12-18 month cycle. If it's six months, the consolidation work matters even more. If it's 36 months, we might be over-rotating."

BAD (performative, rhetorical): "Are we ready for what comes next?"

GOOD (genuine, sharp): "Why is retrieval so much easier to build than consolidation? I've thought about this for a year and the cleanest answer I have is: retrieval has a benchmark. Consolidation doesn't yet. The thing we can grade is the thing we ship."

BAD (performative, transition): "Where do we go from here? The answer lies in context."

## Anti-patterns
- Including a question because the post "needs more engagement." If the question isn't real, the engagement won't be real either.
- Closing every piece with a question. Variety matters; some pieces close with a sharp claim, some with a story moment, some with a real question. Don't default to one.
