# Practitioner, Not Commentator

## What it is
The rule that distinguishes Riché's voice from the broader AI thought leadership cohort. Riché writes from inside the work — as a practitioner who builds, ships, and measures — not from outside as a commentator who summarizes other people's work. Every piece must read as if Riché's hands are on a system that touches the claim being made. Pieces that read like commentary on the AI industry, summary of someone else's framework, or curated takes on the news cycle fail the rule.

## Why it matters
The AI conversation in 2025-2026 is saturated with commentators. People who summarize trends, restate frameworks they read elsewhere, post takes on news without ever shipping a system that touches the problem. Riché's differentiation is that he ships. The rule keeps the voice on the practitioner side of the line; without it, content drifts toward commentary because commentary is faster to produce and the news cycle rewards speed. The discipline of staying on the practitioner side is what compounds the voice over years.

## How to apply
- Every piece must reference at least one built thing — Grandstage, Suzy, Helm Labs, IBM, Context Layer Engine, Boston Beer 2003 — and connect that built thing to the claim being made.
- When tempted to summarize an industry trend or someone else's framework, pause. Either Riché has practitioner experience that lets him extend or challenge the source — in which case extend (see `fatal-fifteen-10-summarizing-vs-extending.md`) — or he doesn't, in which case skip the post.
- Tuesday Signals are the highest-risk format for commentator drift because they react to news. The practitioner-first discipline applies extra hard there: the lens is always context architecture, the proof is always a built system.
- "I've been thinking about" framings can be commentary in disguise. Replace with "We learned this when," "I made this decision when," "We shipped this and found."

## Examples
GOOD (practitioner): "Most AI teams treat data as context. They retrieve more, hoping more becomes meaning. It doesn't. We learned this at Grandstage when 300% growth at $0 CAC came from the upstream consolidation work, not the model."

BAD (commentator): "There's a growing consensus in the AI industry that context is becoming a critical differentiator. Industry analysts have been observing this trend for some time."

GOOD (practitioner): "Suzy shipped the Decision Engine in six weeks because the consolidation layer was already built. 350+ enterprise customers got Intelligence, Insights, Impact on day one. The launch operationalized what was already there."

BAD (commentator): "Recent enterprise AI launches have demonstrated that speed-to-market is increasingly a function of architectural readiness. Many companies are now exploring this approach."

GOOD (practitioner extending a source): "Anthropic's piece on context engineering this week is doing the right work, and it's still missing the consolidation layer. Curate-synthesize-retrieve gets most teams 70% of the way. The remaining 30% — turning retrieved data into decision-ready context — is where production AI products actually fail. We learned this the hard way at Suzy."

BAD (commentator summarizing a source): "Anthropic published a thoughtful piece on context engineering this week. They argue that context is more important than the model and walk through several examples. Worth a read."

## Anti-patterns
- Posting takes on AI news without connecting them to a Riché-built system. The take collapses into commentary; the audience can get the same take from anyone.
- Citing other practitioners' work as if it's evidence for Riché's claim. Other people's work supports a trend; only Riché's own work supports his thesis.
