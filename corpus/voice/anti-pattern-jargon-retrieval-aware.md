# No Jargon Phrases Like "Retrieval-Aware Architectures"

## What it is
Manufactured jargon — "retrieval-aware architectures," "context intelligence," "AI-native paradigms," "semantically optimized" — must not appear in drafts. These phrases sound technical without communicating anything specific; they are decoration disguised as precision. The voice rule replaces them with plain language that names the actual thing the writer is talking about.

## Why it matters
Jargon phrases are the failure mode of authority-without-accessibility. They signal that the writer is trying to sound smart instead of be clear. Worse, manufactured jargon often hides the absence of a real claim — when "retrieval-aware architecture" is unpacked, it usually means "the system retrieves context that's relevant," which is the table-stakes definition of retrieval. Riché's voice avoids the trap by using language a sharp practitioner would actually use in a working session: "consolidation layer," "five-step process," "context system." Plain, specific, useful.

## How to apply
- Search every draft for compound technical-sounding phrases. For each, ask: does this phrase name a real, specific thing? If yes, keep it. If no, replace with plain language.
- "Context intelligence" is not an established term and is banned (see `terminology-never-use.md`). "Context generation," "context synthesis," and "consolidation" are accepted because they name specific operations.
- The test: would a sharp PM at a competing company recognize this phrase as referring to a specific operation? If they'd nod, it's real. If they'd squint, it's jargon.
- Acronyms, model names, and standard CS terms (RAG, embeddings, vector DB) are fine when used precisely. The ban is on manufactured compound buzzwords.

## Examples
BAD: "We're building retrieval-aware architectures for AI-native enterprise paradigms."

GOOD: "We're building the consolidation layer between retrieval and the model. Most enterprise teams skip it."

BAD: "Suzy's Decision Engine leverages context intelligence to deliver semantically optimized insights."

GOOD: "Suzy's Decision Engine surfaces three insights per decision instead of thirty. The consolidation layer ranks by decision relevance, not search relevance."

BAD: "The Context Layer Engine implements a knowledge-aware substrate for context-augmented reasoning."

GOOD: "The Context Layer Engine is a personal knowledge system that runs the same five-step context generation process I use at Grandstage. Curate, synthesize, consolidate, prioritize, store intelligently."

BAD: "Grandstage uses AI-powered, intelligent context optimization."

GOOD: "Grandstage runs a context system on top of market data. It curates inputs, synthesizes across sources, and consolidates to one decision-ready view per ICP."

## Anti-patterns
- Inventing new compound jargon when the existing kind gets banned ("intelligence-augmented retrieval"). Same trap with new wallpaper.
- Defending jargon because it appears in industry analyst reports. Analysts also fall into the manufactured-jargon trap. Independence from analyst register is part of the voice.
