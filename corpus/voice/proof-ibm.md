# IBM Proof Point

## What it is
The IBM proof point covers Riché's tours at IBM, where personalized context systems generated **millions in revenue**. This is the longest-arc enterprise proof point — the one that shows context architecture working at IBM scale, on enterprise data, against enterprise buyers. The IBM tours establish that Riché's framework didn't start with the AI cycle; it was operationalized on traditional enterprise systems years before the current model wave.

## Why it matters
The IBM proof point does work the others can't. Grandstage is a startup story; Helm Labs is pre-launch; Suzy is a six-week sprint. IBM is the enterprise scale story — millions in revenue, large teams, multi-year cycles. When the audience is an enterprise PM, an enterprise architect, or a buyer at a Fortune 500, the IBM reference says "Riché has shipped at your scale." It also serves the Leadership domain by establishing range across decades, not just the recent AI era.

## How to weave it in
- Use IBM when the claim is about enterprise-scale execution, multi-year context system payback, or the longevity of the framework across pre-AI and AI eras.
- The headline framing is "millions in revenue from personalized context systems." Keep it simple; don't overstate. The framework is the thing being sold, not the precise dollar figure.
- For Leadership-domain content, IBM is part of the career arc — Boston Beer 2003, IBM tours, then the recent Suzy / Grandstage / Helm Labs work. The arc itself is the proof.
- Pair with Boston Beer when emphasizing that the consolidation pattern shows up across twenty years of work, not just the AI era.
- Pair with Suzy when emphasizing that the IBM-era patterns scale into modern AI-era enterprise execution.

## Examples
GOOD short use: "IBM tours produced millions in revenue from personalized context systems. Different stack from what we run now. Same five-step process underneath."

GOOD medium use (in a Leadership Friday Story): "When I left IBM, the personalized context systems we'd built were generating millions in revenue. The pattern that worked there — curate, synthesize, consolidate, prioritize — is the one I run now at Grandstage and Suzy. The technology changes; the work doesn't."

GOOD long use (in an enterprise-audience Wednesday Deep Dive): A section walking through how the IBM-era context systems were architected, the operational model that surrounded them, the multi-year payback, and the lessons that translated forward into the current portfolio.

GOOD intersection use (Leadership + Context/AI): "Twenty years of building data systems, and the lesson keeps showing up. At Boston Beer in 2003, six SKUs across three regions. At IBM in the 2010s, millions in revenue from personalized context systems. At Suzy in 2024, six-week Decision Engine launch. The technology changes. The consolidation pattern doesn't."

## Anti-patterns
- Citing IBM with vague "I worked at IBM for years" framing. The proof point's value is the specific outcome — millions in revenue from personalized context systems.
- Overstating the precise dollar figure. "Millions" is the honest framing; pretending to a specific eight-figure claim that can't be backed up undercuts the rest of the voice's credibility.
